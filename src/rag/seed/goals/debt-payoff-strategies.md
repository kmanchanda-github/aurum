# Debt Payoff Strategies

Eliminating debt is one of the highest-return "investments" you can make — paying off a 20% APR credit card is equivalent to earning 20% guaranteed. Choosing the right payoff strategy saves money and builds momentum.

## The Two Main Strategies

### The Avalanche Method (Math Optimal)

Pay the minimum on all debts, then direct all extra money to the debt with the **highest interest rate** first. Once paid off, roll that payment to the next highest rate debt.

**Why it wins mathematically**: You minimize total interest paid over time.

**Example**: 
- Credit card A: $5,000 at 24% APR
- Credit card B: $3,000 at 18% APR
- Car loan: $10,000 at 6% APR

Avalanche order: A → B → Car loan.

Over a typical payoff period, avalanche saves hundreds to thousands in interest vs. other methods.

### The Snowball Method (Psychology Optimal)

Pay the minimum on all debts, then direct extra money to the **smallest balance** first, regardless of interest rate. Knock out small debts quickly for motivation.

**Why it wins behaviorally**: Quick wins generate momentum. Research shows debt-payoff success rates are higher with snowball, even though it costs slightly more in interest.

**Best for**: People who've tried avalanche and lost motivation. The wins matter.

## Which Should You Choose?

- If you're highly motivated and math-driven: **Avalanche**
- If you need quick wins to stay on track: **Snowball**
- If interest rates are all similar: **Snowball** (the psychological benefit costs little)
- If you have a high-rate debt with a large balance: **Avalanche** (the interest savings are too large to ignore)

## High-Rate vs. Low-Rate Debt

Not all debt is created equal. The urgency to pay off debt depends on its interest rate compared to investment returns.

| Debt Type | Typical Rate | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Credit cards | 18–29% | Pay off immediately — guaranteed 18-29% return |
| Personal loans | 8–20% | High priority — match or beat with investments |
| Car loans | 4–8% | Moderate priority — could earn more investing |
| Student loans | 3–7% (federal) | Lower priority — consider income-driven repayment |
| Mortgage | 3–7% | Lowest priority — tax-deductible, likely below long-run stock returns |

## The "Invest or Pay Off Debt?" Decision

When debt rates are moderate (4–7%), the answer is nuanced:

**Pay debt if:**
- The rate is guaranteed; investment returns are not
- The psychological burden of debt affects your wellbeing
- You don't have an emergency fund yet (build that first)

**Invest alongside debt if:**
- Employer offers 401k match (free money — always capture this first)
- Debt rate is below 5–6% and you have a long investment horizon
- You have a stable income and emergency fund

**The hybrid approach**: Pay minimums on low-rate debt, capture employer 401k match, build emergency fund, then aggressively attack high-rate debt.

## Debt Consolidation

Consolidating multiple high-rate debts into one lower-rate loan simplifies payments and saves interest.

**Balance transfer cards**: 0% intro APR for 12–21 months on transferred balances. A transfer fee (3–5%) applies. Must pay off before the promotional period ends or face high rates.

**Personal loan consolidation**: Fixed rate (often 7–15%), fixed term. Predictable payments. Better for those who can't reliably pay off a balance transfer in time.

**Cash-out refinance**: Use home equity to pay off high-rate debt. Risky — you're converting unsecured debt to debt secured by your home.

## Staying Motivated

- Track total debt balance weekly — seeing the number drop is powerful
- Celebrate milestones (every $5,000 paid off)
- Use apps like YNAB or Undebt.it to visualize progress
- Automate extra payments so willpower isn't required monthly
