# 529 Plans: Saving for College Tax-Free

A 529 plan is a tax-advantaged savings account designed for education expenses. Money grows tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified education expenses are also tax-free — a powerful benefit for long-term college savings.

## What Is a 529 Plan?

Named after Section 529 of the IRS code, these are state-sponsored investment accounts. You invest after-tax dollars, which then grow without being taxed annually. When you withdraw for eligible education costs, there's no federal tax.

**Qualified expenses include:** Tuition, fees, books, supplies, room and board (for enrolled students), and computers used for school.

**Starting in 2024:** K-12 tuition (up to $10,000/year) and student loan repayment (up to $10,000 lifetime) also qualify.

## State Tax Benefits

Most states offer a state income tax deduction or credit for 529 contributions, typically up to $10,000–$20,000/year depending on the state. You generally don't have to use your own state's 529 plan, but you may lose the state tax deduction if you choose another state's plan.

## Contribution Limits

No annual contribution limit, but contributions are considered gifts. In 2024, the annual gift tax exclusion is $18,000/person. You can superfund: contribute 5 years' worth at once ($90,000 single / $180,000 married) without triggering gift tax.

Total account limits vary by state, typically $300,000–$550,000.

## Investment Options

529 plans offer mutual funds and ETFs, typically with age-based options that automatically shift conservative as the child approaches college age — similar to target-date retirement funds.

Choose low-expense-ratio index funds when available. The investment compounding happens over 18 years, so costs matter.

## What Happens to Unused Funds?

**Rollover to another beneficiary:** Change the beneficiary to another family member (sibling, cousin, even parent) with no penalty.

**Roth IRA rollover:** Starting in 2024, you can roll up to $35,000 lifetime into a Roth IRA for the beneficiary (account must be open 15+ years; subject to Roth contribution limits).

**Non-qualified withdrawal:** Earnings are subject to income tax plus a 10% penalty. Contributions (your principal) are always yours penalty-free.

## How Much to Save

College costs historically inflate at ~5–6% annually. A common target: save 1/3 of projected costs (the rest from current income, scholarships, and aid). Saving $300–400/month from birth typically covers a 4-year public university.

Use a 529 calculator to project based on your state's average costs and expected investment returns.
