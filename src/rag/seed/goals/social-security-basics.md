# Social Security and Retirement Planning

Social Security is the foundation of retirement income for most Americans. Understanding how benefits are calculated, when to claim, and how they interact with your other savings helps maximize lifetime income.

## How Social Security Works

Social Security is a federal program funded by payroll taxes (6.2% from employee, 6.2% from employer, on income up to $168,600 in 2024). You earn "credits" as you work — 40 credits (roughly 10 years of work) qualifies you for retirement benefits.

Your benefit is based on your **35 highest-earning years**, adjusted for inflation. Years with no earnings count as zero, which drags down the average.

## When Can You Claim?

| Age | Benefit Level |
|---|---|
| 62 | Earliest possible; benefit reduced 25–30% permanently |
| 67 (Full Retirement Age, FRA) | 100% of your calculated benefit |
| 70 | Maximum benefit — 8% more per year past FRA = +24% total |

**Delayed claiming is powerful**: If your FRA benefit is $2,000/month:
- Claim at 62: $1,400/month
- Claim at 67: $2,000/month
- Claim at 70: $2,480/month

Over a 20-year retirement (age 70–90), waiting from 62 to 70 yields roughly $115,000 more in total benefits (breakeven around age 80).

## Break-Even Analysis

The break-even point for claiming early vs. late is typically around age 80.
- If you expect to live past 80–82: delay claiming
- If health is poor or family history suggests shorter lifespan: claim earlier

Married couples have more flexibility — the higher earner should usually delay to maximize the survivor benefit.

## Spousal and Survivor Benefits

**Spousal benefit**: A non-working or lower-earning spouse can claim up to 50% of the higher-earning spouse's benefit at FRA.

**Survivor benefit**: If you die, your spouse can claim 100% of your benefit amount. This is a powerful reason for the higher earner to delay to age 70 — it maximizes the survivor's lifetime income.

## Social Security and Taxes

Social Security benefits may be taxable depending on your "combined income" (AGI + half your Social Security):
- Single filer under $25,000: No tax on benefits
- Single filer $25,000–$34,000: Up to 50% of benefits taxable
- Single filer above $34,000: Up to 85% of benefits taxable
- Married (joint) thresholds: $32,000 and $44,000

Roth IRA withdrawals don't count toward combined income — another reason to do Roth conversions before claiming Social Security.

## Social Security in Your Retirement Plan

Social Security replaces roughly 40% of pre-retirement income for average earners. Most financial planners target 70–90% income replacement in retirement. The gap must come from:
- 401k/IRA/Roth IRA savings
- Pension (if applicable)
- Part-time work
- Other assets

**Rule of thumb**: For every $1,000/month in Social Security income you expect, you need roughly $240,000 less in investment savings (using the 4% rule as the benchmark).

## How to Check Your Benefit Estimate

Create an account at SSA.gov to view your Social Security statement. It shows:
- Your estimated benefit at 62, 67, and 70
- Your complete earnings history
- Your survivor and disability benefit estimates

Review it annually and correct any errors in your earnings record promptly.

*Note: Social Security rules are complex and subject to legislative change. Consult a certified financial planner for advice specific to your situation.*
