# Saving for a Car

Buying a car is one of the largest purchases most people make — second only to a home. The financing decision alone can cost (or save) tens of thousands of dollars over a vehicle's lifetime. Here's a practical framework for planning and saving.

## The True Cost of a Car

The sticker price is just the beginning. Total ownership costs include:

| Cost | Annual Estimate |
|---|---|
| Depreciation | $2,000–$5,000 (new), $1,000–$2,500 (used) |
| Insurance | $1,200–$2,400 |
| Fuel | $1,500–$3,000 |
| Maintenance & repairs | $500–$1,500 |
| Registration/taxes | $200–$800 |
| **Total (new mid-size car)** | **~$8,000–$12,000/year** |

The 20-4-10 Rule of thumb:
- **20%+ down payment**
- **4 years or less** for the loan term
- **10% or less** of gross monthly income on total car expenses

## New vs. Used: The Depreciation Math

New cars lose 15–25% of their value in the first year. A $35,000 car is worth ~$26,000 after year 1.

Buying a 2–3 year old certified pre-owned (CPO) vehicle lets someone else absorb that depreciation hit. You often get a nearly-new car with the manufacturer warranty for 25–30% less.

**Best value sweet spot**: 2–4 year old vehicles with 25,000–50,000 miles.

## How Much Car Can You Afford?

**Conservative rule**: Total car payment (loan + insurance) ≤ 15% of monthly take-home pay.

**Example**: $4,000/month take-home → max car payment + insurance = $600/month.

If insurance runs $150/month, that leaves $450 for the loan payment. At 6% APR over 48 months, $450/month supports a loan of ~$19,000.

Add a $5,000 down payment → total car budget of ~$24,000.

## Saving for a Down Payment

**Step 1**: Set a target purchase price and timeline.
Example: $25,000 car, buy in 18 months, want 20% down ($5,000).

**Step 2**: Monthly savings needed = $5,000 ÷ 18 = ~$278/month.

**Step 3**: Where to save:
- **High-yield savings account (HYSA)**: 4–5% APY, fully liquid, FDIC insured. Best for goals under 2–3 years.
- **Money market fund**: Slightly higher yield, also liquid.
- **CDs**: Lock in a rate if you know exactly when you'll buy.
- **Do NOT invest in stocks** for a short-term goal — market can be down 30% when you need the money.

## Financing Options

**New car dealer financing**: Often competitive, especially during promotions (0% APR deals from manufacturers). Read the fine print — 0% may come with higher purchase price or fewer negotiating options.

**Credit union loans**: Often lower rates than banks or dealers. Apply before visiting a dealership to know your rate.

**Bank auto loans**: Pre-approval gives you negotiating power at the dealership.

**Online lenders**: LightStream, PenFed, Capital One Auto — competitive rates, quick approval.

**Avoid**:
- 72–84 month loans (you'll be "underwater" on the car for years)
- Dealer add-ons (extended warranties, paint protection, gap insurance — overpriced at the dealership)

## Timing Your Purchase

- **Best months to buy**: October–December (dealers hitting year-end quotas)
- **Best days**: Last day of the month (salespeople rushing to meet monthly quotas)
- **End of model year**: August–September when new models arrive, dealers discount outgoing inventory

## Electric vs. Gasoline

Electric vehicles (EVs) have higher upfront costs but lower operating costs (no oil changes, cheaper electricity vs. gas). Federal EV tax credits (up to $7,500) can dramatically reduce the effective price. Check eligibility based on vehicle price, your income, and whether you buy new or used.

## Practical Takeaway

The best car purchase is one you can comfortably afford, with at least 20% down, a loan term of 48 months or less, and total car costs under 15% of take-home pay. Buying 2–4 years used with cash or a credit union loan beats financing a new car in almost every scenario.
