# Retirement Planning: How Much Do You Need?

Retirement planning is the process of determining how much money you'll need to stop working and funding that goal through saving and investing.

## The 4% Rule

The most widely used retirement withdrawal rule: you can safely withdraw 4% of your portfolio per year in retirement, adjusted for inflation, without running out of money over a 30-year retirement.

To find your retirement number: multiply your desired annual retirement spending by 25.

**Examples:**
- Annual spending of $50,000/year → need $1,250,000
- Annual spending of $80,000/year → need $2,000,000
- Annual spending of $100,000/year → need $2,500,000

The 4% rule emerged from the "Trinity Study" which backtested withdrawal rates against historical market data. More conservative planners use 3–3.5% for a 40+ year retirement.

## How Long Until Retirement? — The Savings Rate Equation

Your savings rate is the biggest determinant of how quickly you can retire.

| Savings Rate | Years to Retirement (from $0) |
|---|---|
| 10% | ~40 years |
| 25% | ~27 years |
| 50% | ~17 years |
| 70% | ~8 years |

Assumptions: 5% real return (7% nominal minus 2% inflation), spending 100% minus savings rate in retirement.

## Social Security

For most Americans, Social Security replaces 30–40% of pre-retirement income. You can claim at 62 (reduced), 67 (full retirement age for those born after 1960), or 70 (maximum benefit — 76% more than at 62).

Delaying Social Security to 70 is often the best strategy for healthy individuals with other income sources. Each year of delay after 62 increases the benefit by ~6–8%.

## Monte Carlo Projections

Simple calculators assume fixed returns, but real markets are volatile. Monte Carlo simulations run thousands of scenarios with random return sequences to estimate probability of success.

A 90% probability of success means your plan works in 9 out of 10 historical market scenarios — generally considered a reasonable target.

## The Sequence-of-Returns Risk

Retiring into a bear market is far more damaging than a bear market in mid-career. A 40% portfolio drop in your first year of retirement, combined with withdrawals, can permanently impair your portfolio.

Mitigation strategies:
- Hold 1–2 years of expenses in cash/short bonds ("bucket strategy")
- Reduce withdrawals temporarily during downturns
- Part-time work for the first few years of retirement

## Healthcare Costs

Healthcare is the biggest wildcard in retirement planning. Before Medicare eligibility at 65, retirees must obtain their own coverage. Average healthcare costs in retirement can exceed $300,000 per couple over a 30-year retirement.

Build healthcare cost projections into your retirement number and consider an HSA as a dedicated healthcare savings vehicle.
