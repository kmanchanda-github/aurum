# Saving for a House Down Payment

A home down payment is typically the largest single savings goal before retirement. Strategic planning makes it achievable without sacrificing other financial priorities.

## How Much Do You Need?

**Conventional loan (best rates):** 20% down payment avoids Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI), saving 0.5–1.5% of the loan amount per year.

**FHA loan:** 3.5% down with a credit score ≥ 580. Requires mortgage insurance for the life of the loan (unless refinanced).

**Conventional with PMI:** 3–10% down. PMI is removed once you reach 20% equity.

**VA loans:** 0% down for eligible veterans and service members. No PMI.

For a $400,000 home:
- 20% down: $80,000
- 10% down: $40,000
- 3.5% down: $14,000

Don't forget closing costs (2–5% of purchase price) and a cash reserve post-purchase.

## Where to Keep Down Payment Savings

Unlike retirement savings (decades away), a down payment has a short time horizon (1–5 years). This changes where you should save:

**0–2 years:** High-yield savings accounts (HYSA) or money market accounts. Currently earning 4–5%+ APY. Capital preservation is paramount — you can't afford a stock market decline to wipe out your savings right before buying.

**3–5 years:** Consider a mix: keep most in HYSA, perhaps 20–30% in short-term bond funds. Avoid heavy stock exposure.

**5+ years:** Can include some stock exposure, but reduce to conservative allocation 2–3 years before your target date.

## Down Payment Savings Strategies

**Set a specific target and date:** "Save $60,000 by January 2027" is more actionable than "save for a house someday."

**Automate savings:** Set up automatic transfers on payday. Treat the savings like a bill payment.

**First-time homebuyer IRA withdrawal:** You can withdraw up to $10,000 penalty-free from a Traditional or Roth IRA for a first home purchase (taxes still apply on Traditional IRA withdrawals).

**Roth IRA principal access:** Roth IRA contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn tax- and penalty-free anytime. Some use Roth IRAs as dual-purpose retirement/down payment accounts.

## Rent vs. Buy Calculation

Buying isn't always better than renting. Key factors:
- Price-to-rent ratio: If home prices are more than 20× annual rent for comparable housing, renting may make more financial sense.
- Time horizon: Generally need to stay at least 5 years to recover transaction costs.
- Opportunity cost: Down payment invested in the stock market vs. locked in home equity.

Use a comprehensive rent vs. buy calculator (New York Times has a well-regarded one) before deciding.
