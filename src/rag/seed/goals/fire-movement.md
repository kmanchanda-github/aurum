# The FIRE Movement Explained

FIRE stands for Financial Independence, Retire Early. It's a movement built around aggressive saving, frugal living, and strategic investing to achieve financial freedom decades before traditional retirement age. While FIRE has roots in extreme frugality, modern versions offer more flexibility.

## The Core Math: The 4% Rule

The FIRE movement relies on the **4% rule** (also called the Safe Withdrawal Rate). The rule states:

If you withdraw 4% of your portfolio annually in retirement, your portfolio will last 30+ years in the vast majority of historical scenarios.

**The formula:**
**FIRE Number = Annual Expenses × 25**

Examples:
- Spend $40,000/year → FIRE Number = $1,000,000
- Spend $60,000/year → FIRE Number = $1,500,000
- Spend $100,000/year → FIRE Number = $2,500,000

This is based on the **Trinity Study** (1998), which backtested different withdrawal rates over 30-year periods using historical market data.

## Variants of FIRE

**Lean FIRE**: Retire on a very minimal budget (under $30,000–$40,000/year). Requires extreme frugality and a smaller nest egg. Higher risk if expenses rise.

**Fat FIRE**: Retire with a comfortable or luxurious budget ($80,000–$150,000+/year). Requires $2M–$4M+. More cushion but takes longer to achieve.

**Barista FIRE**: Reach partial financial independence, then work part-time or do low-stress work to cover some expenses. The portfolio doesn't need to cover 100% of costs.

**Coast FIRE**: Save enough early that compound growth alone will reach your FIRE number by traditional retirement age — without contributing another dollar. Allows you to work less or in lower-paying fulfilling jobs without touching investments.

## The Savings Rate Is Everything

The key driver of FIRE timelines isn't income — it's **savings rate** (savings ÷ income).

| Savings Rate | Years to FIRE (from zero) |
|---|---|
| 10% | ~43 years |
| 25% | ~32 years |
| 50% | ~17 years |
| 65% | ~11 years |
| 75% | ~7 years |

Assumes 7% real investment return, spending same in retirement as working years.

## How FIRE Investors Invest

FIRE adherents typically invest in:
- **Low-cost broad index funds** (VTI, VXUS, BND)
- **Tax-advantaged accounts first**: max 401k, IRA, HSA before taxable
- **Roth conversion ladder**: convert traditional IRA to Roth over time to access funds penalty-free before age 59.5

## Risks and Criticisms

**Sequence of returns risk**: Retiring into a bear market can devastate a portfolio. If stocks fall 40% in year 1 and you're withdrawing 4%, you may not recover.

**Healthcare**: Before Medicare at 65, health insurance is a major expense. FIRE retirees need to budget $500–$1,500+/month.

**Lifestyle creep**: Expenses often rise with life events (children, health issues, aging parents). A "4% rule" portfolio may be too thin.

**Longevity risk**: Retiring at 35 means your portfolio needs to last 50–60 years, not 30. Some advocate a 3–3.5% withdrawal rate for very early retirees.

**Meaning and purpose**: Many FIRE practitioners discover that "retirement" from meaningful work isn't the goal — financial freedom to choose your work is.

## Practical First Steps

1. Calculate your current annual spending
2. Multiply by 25 to find your FIRE number
3. Calculate your savings rate
4. Open and max out tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA, HSA)
5. Invest in low-cost index funds
6. Track net worth monthly

FIRE is a spectrum, not an all-or-nothing goal. Even pursuing partial FIRE principles dramatically improves financial resilience and optionality.
