# Life Insurance Basics

Life insurance is a financial planning tool designed to protect the people who depend on your income if you die prematurely. Understanding what type you need — and how much — prevents both underinsurance (leaving your family vulnerable) and overinsurance (paying for coverage you don't need).

## Who Needs Life Insurance?

Life insurance is essential if people depend on your income:
- Spouse or partner who would struggle financially without your income
- Children (until they're financially independent)
- Parents or other dependents you support financially

Life insurance is generally NOT needed if:
- You're single with no dependents
- You're retired with sufficient savings and a pension/Social Security that covers expenses
- Your assets already cover your family's needs if you died

## The Two Main Types

### Term Life Insurance

Covers you for a specific period (10, 20, or 30 years). If you die during the term, your beneficiaries receive the death benefit. If you outlive the term, coverage ends and you paid for protection that wasn't needed — but that's the point.

**Why term is usually the right choice**:
- Pure insurance — no investment component
- Much cheaper: a healthy 35-year-old can get $1M of coverage for $30–50/month
- Covers the years when you actually need it (while kids are young, mortgage is large)

**Drawbacks**: Coverage ends. As you age and apply again, premiums rise significantly.

### Permanent Life Insurance (Whole/Universal)

Covers you for life. Builds a "cash value" investment component alongside the death benefit. Much more expensive — often 5–15x the cost of equivalent term coverage.

**When permanent insurance might make sense**:
- Estate planning for very high-net-worth individuals (uses life insurance to pay estate taxes)
- Business succession planning
- Special needs dependents who will always rely on support

**Avoid permanent insurance if**:
- An agent leads with investment returns — life insurance is rarely the best investment vehicle
- You can't clearly explain why you need lifetime coverage vs. term

## How Much Do You Need?

Several methods to estimate:

**DIME Method**: 
- **D**ebt (all debts you'd leave behind)
- **I**ncome (annual income × years until youngest child is independent)
- **M**ortgage (remaining balance)
- **E**ducation (college cost for each child)

**Simple Rule**: 10–12× annual income as a starting point.

**Example**: Earn $80,000/year with a $300,000 mortgage, two kids, and $50,000 in other debt:
- Income replacement: $80,000 × 20 = $1,600,000
- Mortgage: $300,000
- Education: $200,000 (two kids)
- Debt: $50,000
- **Total estimated need: ~$2.1M**

Reduce by existing savings and any workplace group life insurance.

## Term Length

- **While kids are young**: 20–30 year term started in your late 20s/early 30s
- **Mortgage coverage**: Match term to mortgage payoff (15–20 years)
- **Income replacement until retirement**: 20–30 year term to cover your working years

## Where to Buy

**Online term quotes**: Policygenius, SelectQuote, NerdWallet let you compare quotes from multiple insurers. Most people need to get a medical exam (blood draw, basic health questions).

**No-exam policies**: Available for smaller amounts (under $500K) or for people with health concerns. Higher premiums but faster approval.

**Group life through employer**: Often 1–2× salary, may be portable. Usually not enough and shouldn't substitute for individual policy.

## Key Terms

- **Death benefit**: Amount paid to beneficiaries
- **Premium**: Monthly/annual cost
- **Beneficiary**: Person(s) who receive the death benefit
- **Underwriting**: The insurer's process of assessing your health risk to set your premium
