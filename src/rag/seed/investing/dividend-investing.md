# Dividend Investing

Dividend investing focuses on buying stocks that pay regular cash distributions to shareholders. It's a strategy popular with income-seeking investors and those who prefer a tangible return while they wait for long-term growth.

## What Is a Dividend?

A dividend is a portion of a company's profits paid out to shareholders, usually quarterly. If you own 100 shares of a company that pays $1.00 per share annually, you receive $100 per year in dividends regardless of whether the stock price moves.

## Key Dividend Metrics

**Dividend Yield**: Annual dividend per share ÷ current stock price.
- A stock at $50 paying $2/year has a 4% yield
- Average S&P 500 yield is historically ~1.5–2%

**Payout Ratio**: Dividends paid ÷ earnings per share.
- A payout ratio below 60% is generally sustainable
- Above 80% may signal the dividend is at risk of being cut

**Dividend Growth Rate**: How fast the company increases its dividend each year.
- "Dividend Aristocrats" are S&P 500 companies that have raised dividends for 25+ consecutive years

## Why Investors Choose Dividend Stocks

**Steady income**: Dividends provide cash flow without needing to sell shares — useful in retirement.

**Compounding via reinvestment**: Reinvesting dividends (DRIP programs) buys more shares, which pay more dividends, creating a compounding snowball.

**Inflation hedge**: Companies that grow dividends annually often keep pace with or beat inflation.

**Signal of financial health**: Only consistently profitable companies can sustain rising dividends.

## Reinvestment Math

$10,000 invested in a stock with a 3% yield growing dividends 6% annually:
- After 20 years with reinvestment: ~$38,000+
- After 20 years without reinvestment: ~$27,000

The difference is the dividend compounding effect.

## Risks of Dividend Investing

**Dividend cuts**: Companies cut dividends during financial stress. A high yield can be a warning sign, not a gift — the market may already expect a cut.

**Concentration risk**: High-dividend sectors (utilities, real estate, consumer staples) may dominate a dividend portfolio, reducing diversification.

**Tax drag**: In taxable accounts, qualified dividends are taxed at 0–20%. In tax-deferred accounts (IRA, 401k), dividends grow tax-free until withdrawal.

## High Yield vs. Dividend Growth

- **High yield now**: REITs, utilities, MLPs — yields of 4–8%, but slower stock price growth
- **Dividend growth**: Tech and consumer companies paying 1–2% but raising dividends 10–15% yearly — lower yield today, much higher in 10 years

Many investors combine both for current income and future income growth.

## Practical Starting Points

- **ETFs**: VYM (Vanguard High Dividend Yield), SCHD (Schwab US Dividend Equity), VIG (Dividend Appreciation)
- **Individual stocks**: Look for payout ratios under 60%, 10+ years of consecutive increases, and strong free cash flow
