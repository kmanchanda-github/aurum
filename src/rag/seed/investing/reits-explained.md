# REITs Explained

Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) let ordinary investors own income-producing real estate without becoming a landlord. They trade on stock exchanges like regular shares and are required by law to distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends.

## How REITs Work

A REIT is a company that owns, operates, or finances real estate. Investors buy shares, and the REIT uses that capital to acquire and manage properties. Rental income, mortgage interest, and property sales generate the revenue that gets distributed as dividends.

## Types of REITs

**Equity REITs**: Own and operate physical properties — apartment buildings, shopping malls, office towers, warehouses, data centers, cell towers. This is the most common type.

**Mortgage REITs (mREITs)**: Lend money to real estate owners or invest in mortgage-backed securities. They profit from the spread between short-term borrowing costs and long-term mortgage rates. Higher risk, higher yield.

**Hybrid REITs**: Combine both equity and mortgage strategies.

**By Sector:**
- Residential (apartment complexes)
- Commercial (offices, retail)
- Industrial (warehouses, logistics)
- Healthcare (hospitals, senior housing)
- Data Centers (server farms)
- Self-Storage
- Cell Towers / Infrastructure

## Why Invest in REITs?

**High dividend yields**: Because REITs must pay out 90%+ of income, yields of 3–6% are typical — higher than most stocks.

**Real estate exposure without being a landlord**: No maintenance calls, no tenants, no property management.

**Liquidity**: Unlike direct real estate, REIT shares can be bought and sold instantly on a stock exchange.

**Inflation hedge**: Many REIT leases include rent escalation clauses tied to inflation.

**Portfolio diversification**: Real estate often has lower correlation to stocks and bonds.

## Risks

**Interest rate sensitivity**: When rates rise, REIT dividends become less attractive relative to bonds, and REITs often fall in price. Rising rates also increase borrowing costs for property acquisitions.

**Sector-specific risks**: Office REITs have faced headwinds from remote work; retail REITs from e-commerce. Not all sectors behave the same.

**Tax treatment**: REIT dividends are typically taxed as ordinary income, not at the lower qualified dividend rate. Holding REITs in tax-advantaged accounts (IRA, 401k) avoids this.

## How to Invest in REITs

**REIT ETFs (simplest):**
- VNQ — Vanguard Real Estate ETF (broad US REITs)
- SCHH — Schwab US REIT ETF (lower cost)
- VNQI — International REITs

**Individual REITs**: Research specific companies (e.g., Prologis for industrial, Realty Income for retail, American Tower for cell towers).

## REITs in a Portfolio

Many target-date funds and balanced portfolios include a 5–10% allocation to REITs for diversification and income. They sit between stocks and bonds in terms of risk and return.
