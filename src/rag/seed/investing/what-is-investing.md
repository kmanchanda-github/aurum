# What Is Investing?

Investing is the act of allocating money or resources with the expectation of generating income or profit over time. Unlike saving — which simply preserves money — investing puts your money to work, allowing it to potentially grow faster than inflation.

## Why People Invest

The primary reason people invest is to build wealth over time. When you keep money in a savings account, it earns a small interest rate (often below inflation). Investing in assets like stocks, bonds, or real estate historically provides higher long-term returns.

**The power of compound returns**: When your investments generate returns, those returns also generate returns. Over decades, this compounding effect dramatically accelerates wealth accumulation.

Example: $10,000 invested at 7% average annual return becomes:
- After 10 years: ~$19,672
- After 20 years: ~$38,697
- After 30 years: ~$76,123

## Common Investment Vehicles

**Stocks**: Ownership shares in a company. When the company grows, your shares become more valuable. Stocks offer the highest long-term return potential but also the most short-term volatility.

**Bonds**: Loans you make to governments or corporations in exchange for regular interest payments. Generally safer than stocks but with lower returns.

**ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)**: Baskets of securities that trade on exchanges like stocks. Allow instant diversification at low cost.

**Mutual Funds**: Professionally managed portfolios pooling money from many investors. Higher fees than ETFs but sometimes offer active management.

**Real Estate**: Property investment either directly or through REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts).

## Key Principles

1. **Start early** — Time in the market beats timing the market
2. **Diversify** — Don't put all eggs in one basket
3. **Keep costs low** — Fees compound just like returns, but negatively
4. **Stay the course** — Short-term volatility is normal; long-term trends matter
5. **Invest regularly** — Dollar-cost averaging reduces timing risk
