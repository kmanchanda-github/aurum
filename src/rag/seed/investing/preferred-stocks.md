# Preferred Stocks Explained

Preferred stocks sit between common stocks and bonds in the capital structure. They offer some features of both: fixed dividend payments like a bond, but ownership in a company like a stock. Understanding preferred shares helps investors build more nuanced income portfolios.

## How Preferred Stocks Differ from Common Stocks

| Feature | Common Stock | Preferred Stock |
|---|---|---|
| Dividends | Variable, not guaranteed | Fixed rate, paid first |
| Voting rights | Yes | Usually no |
| Priority in bankruptcy | Last to be paid | Before common shareholders |
| Price appreciation | High potential | Limited |
| Risk | Higher | Lower than common, higher than bonds |

## Types of Preferred Stock

**Cumulative Preferred**: If the company misses a dividend payment, the unpaid dividends accumulate. They must be paid to preferred shareholders before common stockholders receive anything.

**Non-Cumulative Preferred**: Missed dividends are gone — they don't accumulate. More risk for the investor.

**Convertible Preferred**: Can be converted into a fixed number of common shares at the investor's discretion. Offers upside if the common stock rises significantly.

**Callable Preferred**: The company can "call back" (redeem) the shares at a fixed price after a specified date — similar to callable bonds. Limits your upside if interest rates fall.

**Participating Preferred**: Entitled to extra dividends if the company's earnings exceed a certain level. Rare in public markets.

## Why Companies Issue Preferred Stock

Preferred dividends are not tax-deductible (unlike bond interest), but preferred stock doesn't dilute common shareholders' voting control the way issuing more common stock would. Banks and utilities frequently issue preferred shares.

## What Preferred Stock Looks Like

A typical preferred share might be issued at $25 par value with a 6% fixed dividend, meaning it pays $1.50/year per share. If interest rates rise and new preferred shares are issued at 7%, your 6% preferred falls in price toward ~$21 to align yields.

## Tax Treatment

Qualified preferred dividends are taxed at the lower capital gains rate (0–20%), not ordinary income rates. This makes preferred shares more tax-efficient than bonds in taxable accounts.

## Risks

**Interest rate risk**: Like bonds, preferred prices fall when interest rates rise.

**Call risk**: Companies often call preferred shares when rates fall, forcing you to reinvest at lower yields.

**Credit risk**: If the company struggles, preferred dividends may be suspended before the company defaults.

**Illiquidity**: Some preferred shares have thin trading volume.

## How to Invest

- **Individual preferred shares**: Trade on exchanges (symbols often include a suffix like .PR or /P)
- **ETFs**: PFF (iShares Preferred and Income Securities ETF), PGX (Invesco Preferred ETF)

## Who Should Consider Preferred Stocks?

Preferred stocks appeal to income-focused investors who want higher yields than bonds but more safety than common stocks. They're most useful in tax-advantaged accounts to shelter the income from tax, and work best as a small portion (5–10%) of a diversified income portfolio.
