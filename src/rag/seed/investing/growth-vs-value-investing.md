# Growth vs. Value Investing

Two of the most enduring investment philosophies are growth investing and value investing. Understanding both helps you decide which approach — or combination — fits your goals and temperament.

## What Is Value Investing?

Value investing means buying stocks that appear to trade below their intrinsic worth. Value investors search for companies the market has temporarily overlooked or punished unfairly.

**Key metrics value investors use:**
- **Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio**: A low P/E relative to peers suggests the stock may be undervalued
- **Price-to-Book (P/B) ratio**: Compares market price to the company's net assets
- **Dividend yield**: Value stocks often pay above-average dividends
- **Free cash flow**: Strong cash generation at a low price is a classic value signal

Famous practitioners: Warren Buffett, Benjamin Graham, Charlie Munger.

**Example**: A bank stock trading at 8x earnings while the sector average is 14x might be a value opportunity — if the business fundamentals are sound.

## What Is Growth Investing?

Growth investing means buying companies expected to grow revenues and earnings faster than the market average, even if they currently trade at high valuations. Investors pay a premium today for superior future earnings.

**Key characteristics of growth stocks:**
- Revenue growing 15–30%+ annually
- Often reinvest all profits rather than paying dividends
- Higher P/E ratios (sometimes 30–100x or more)
- Typically technology, biotech, or disruptive consumer companies

Famous practitioners: Peter Lynch, Cathie Wood, Philip Fisher.

**Example**: A software company growing revenue 40% per year with no dividends but a P/E of 60 is a classic growth stock.

## Key Differences

| Factor | Value | Growth |
|---|---|---|
| Valuation | Low (cheap relative to earnings) | High (premium for future growth) |
| Dividends | Common | Rare |
| Risk | Lower volatility, but risk of "value traps" | Higher volatility |
| Time horizon | Medium to long | Long (5–10+ years) |
| Returns in bull markets | Lags growth | Outperforms |
| Returns in bear markets | Holds better | Falls harder |

## Which Performs Better?

Historically, value stocks have outperformed over very long periods (decades), but growth stocks dominated from 2010 to 2021. Performance rotates — when interest rates rise, growth stocks suffer more because their future earnings get discounted more heavily.

## Blend Investing

Most investors don't need to choose sides. A "blend" approach — owning both value and growth stocks through broad index funds — captures the performance of the full market regardless of which style is leading. Total market index funds (like VTI) naturally hold both.

## Practical Takeaway

If you enjoy researching individual companies, pick a style that matches your temperament. If you prefer simplicity, a low-cost total market index fund gives you both at once with no stock-picking required.
