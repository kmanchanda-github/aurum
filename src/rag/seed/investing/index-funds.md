# Index Funds: The Case for Passive Investing

An index fund is a type of mutual fund or exchange-traded fund (ETF) that tracks a specific market index, such as the S&P 500 or the Nasdaq-100. Rather than relying on a fund manager to pick individual stocks, index funds simply hold every (or a representative sample of) security in the index.

## How Index Funds Work

When you buy shares of an S&P 500 index fund, your money is spread across all 500 companies in the index proportional to their market capitalization. If Apple makes up 7% of the S&P 500, then 7% of your investment goes into Apple stock.

The fund rebalances automatically when the index changes its composition — when companies are added, removed, or when relative weights shift due to price changes.

## The Cost Advantage

Active fund managers charge higher fees — often 0.5% to 1.5% per year — to pay for research analysts and portfolio management. Index funds, by contrast, require minimal management and typically charge expense ratios of 0.03% to 0.20% per year.

This difference compounds dramatically over time. On a $100,000 investment over 30 years at 8% annual returns:
- **Active fund (1% fee):** ~$761,000
- **Index fund (0.05% fee):** ~$993,000

The fee difference alone costs over $230,000.

## Performance Track Record

Academic research consistently shows that the majority of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index over long periods after fees. The SPIVA (S&P Indices vs. Active) report regularly shows that 80–90% of large-cap active managers underperform the S&P 500 over 15-year periods.

This happens for several reasons:
- Markets are largely efficient — prices already reflect available information
- Active managers must overcome their higher fee hurdle
- Behavioral biases lead to poor timing decisions

## Types of Index Funds

**Broad market index funds** track the total stock market (e.g., VTI, FSKAX).

**S&P 500 funds** track the 500 largest US companies (e.g., VOO, FXAIX, SPY).

**Bond index funds** track government or corporate bond markets (e.g., BND, AGG).

**International index funds** track stocks outside the US (e.g., VXUS, EFA).

**Sector index funds** track specific industries like technology or healthcare.

## Getting Started

1. Open a brokerage account (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab, etc.)
2. Decide on your asset allocation (stocks vs. bonds)
3. Choose low-cost index funds matching your allocation
4. Set up automatic contributions
5. Rebalance annually to maintain your target allocation

Index funds are particularly powerful in tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA) where the tax drag on dividends and capital gains is eliminated.
