# Robo-Advisors Explained

Robo-advisors are automated investment platforms that build and manage a diversified portfolio for you based on your goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon. They use algorithms to handle tasks that traditionally required a human financial advisor.

## How Robo-Advisors Work

1. **Onboarding questionnaire**: You answer questions about your age, income, goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon.
2. **Portfolio construction**: The algorithm selects a mix of low-cost ETFs (stocks, bonds, sometimes REITs) suited to your profile.
3. **Automatic rebalancing**: When market movements shift your allocation away from target, the robo-advisor rebalances back automatically.
4. **Tax-loss harvesting**: Premium tiers sell losing positions to offset gains and reduce your tax bill.
5. **Dividend reinvestment**: Dividends are automatically reinvested.

## Costs

Robo-advisors charge an **advisory fee** (typically 0.25% per year) plus the **expense ratios** of the underlying ETFs (typically 0.03–0.15%). Total cost is usually 0.30–0.50% per year — far less than a human advisor's typical 1% fee.

On $100,000: the difference between 0.40% and 1.0% costs is $600/year, which compounds to tens of thousands over decades.

## Pros

- **Hands-off**: Set it and forget it — no stock picking, no rebalancing decisions
- **Low cost**: Much cheaper than traditional advisors
- **Low minimums**: Many start with $0
- **Behavioral guard rails**: Removes emotional decision-making during market crashes
- **Tax efficiency**: Automatic tax-loss harvesting available on most platforms

## Cons

- **Less personalization**: Cannot handle complex situations (business ownership, stock options, estate planning)
- **No human relationship**: No one to call during a market crash who knows your full situation
- **Cash drag**: Some platforms keep 6–10% in cash as a "buffer," which can reduce returns
- **Limited flexibility**: You can't usually pick individual stocks or bonds

## Who Should Use a Robo-Advisor?

Robo-advisors are ideal for:
- Beginners who don't want to pick funds manually
- People with straightforward finances who want automation
- Investors with $1,000–$500,000 who don't yet need complex financial planning

As wealth grows or financial situations become more complex (business sale, inheritance, divorce), a human fee-only financial planner may add more value.

## Alternative: DIY with Index Funds

If you're comfortable selecting 2-3 index funds yourself (e.g., a US total market fund + international + bond fund), you can replicate most robo-advisor portfolios at even lower cost with no advisory fee.
