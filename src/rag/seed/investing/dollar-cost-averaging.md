# Dollar-Cost Averaging: Investing on Autopilot

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is an investment strategy where you invest a fixed amount of money at regular intervals — regardless of the market price. Instead of trying to time the market, you invest consistently over time.

## How It Works

Suppose you invest $500 per month into an S&P 500 index fund:
- When prices are high, $500 buys fewer shares
- When prices are low, $500 buys more shares
- Over time, your average cost per share tends to be lower than the average price

**Example over 4 months:**
| Month | Share Price | Shares Purchased | Total Shares |
|-------|------------|-----------------|--------------|
| 1     | $50        | 10.0            | 10.0         |
| 2     | $40        | 12.5            | 22.5         |
| 3     | $45        | 11.1            | 33.6         |
| 4     | $55        | 9.1             | 42.7         |

Total invested: $2,000. Average price: $47.50. Your average cost: $46.84. You bought at a lower average cost than the average price.

## Psychological Benefits

The biggest benefit of DCA may be behavioral. Markets are volatile and unpredictable. Investing a lump sum can feel terrifying when markets are near highs. DCA removes the emotional burden of timing decisions.

When markets fall, DCA investors automatically buy more shares. This reframing — market dips as buying opportunities — helps investors stay the course.

## DCA vs. Lump Sum Investing

Research shows that lump sum investing (investing all at once) outperforms DCA roughly two-thirds of the time, because markets tend to go up over time and money invested earlier has more time to compound.

However, if investing a lump sum would lead you to panic and sell during a downturn, DCA is the better choice. The best strategy is the one you'll actually stick to.

## Practical Implementation

Most brokerages offer automatic investment plans. Set up a recurring transfer from your bank account to your investment account, then a recurring purchase of your chosen fund. This happens without any effort on your part.

This works especially well with 401(k) contributions, where a percentage of each paycheck is automatically invested — classic dollar-cost averaging.
