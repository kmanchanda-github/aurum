# Passive vs. Active Investing

One of the most consequential investing decisions you'll make isn't which stock to pick — it's whether to pick stocks at all. The passive vs. active debate has decades of data behind it and the answer matters for your long-term returns.

## Active Investing

Active investing means trying to beat the market by selecting specific stocks, bonds, or other assets — or timing when to buy and sell. This includes:
- Individual stock picking
- Actively managed mutual funds
- Tactical asset allocation (moving in and out of sectors)
- Market timing strategies

The goal: outperform a benchmark index (like the S&P 500).

## Passive Investing

Passive investing means owning the entire market (or a broad slice of it) through index funds or ETFs, accepting the market's return rather than trying to beat it.

- Buy-and-hold index funds
- Target-date funds
- Total market ETFs

The goal: match the market's return as cheaply as possible, then let compounding do the work.

## The Data: Who Actually Wins?

**SPIVA (S&P Indices vs. Active) Report** consistently shows:
- Over 15 years, ~90% of actively managed US large-cap funds underperform the S&P 500
- After fees, roughly 95% of active funds trail their benchmark over 20 years

Why? Because markets are informationally efficient — prices already reflect most available information. Consistently finding mispriced assets is extremely difficult, and trading costs erode any edge.

## The Cost Difference

| Fund Type | Average Annual Fee |
|---|---|
| Actively managed mutual fund | 0.5–1.2% |
| Actively managed hedge fund | 1.5–2% + 20% of profits |
| Passive index ETF | 0.03–0.20% |

On $100,000 over 30 years at 7% market return:
- Active fund (1% fee): ~$574,000
- Passive index (0.05% fee): ~$757,000

The difference — ~$183,000 — is the cost of trying to beat the market.

## When Active Might Make Sense

- **Niche markets**: Some less-efficient markets (small-cap, emerging markets, high-yield bonds) may offer more active opportunity
- **Tax-managed accounts**: Skilled active managers can sometimes add value through tax-loss harvesting in taxable accounts
- **Alternative assets**: Private equity, venture capital, and real estate require active management by nature

## The Behavioral Argument for Passive

Active investors tend to trade more, react to news, and make emotional decisions — all of which hurt returns. Passive investors who commit to a simple index fund and ignore short-term noise tend to outperform active investors even when active managers perform well, because passive investors stay invested through downturns.

## Practical Recommendation

For most investors, especially beginners, a core passive portfolio makes sense:
- US total stock market index (e.g., VTI)
- International index (e.g., VXUS)
- Bond index (e.g., BND)

If you enjoy researching companies, allocate a small "play" portion (5–10% of portfolio) to individual stocks. This satisfies the urge to be active without jeopardizing your core returns.
