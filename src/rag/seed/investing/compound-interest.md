# Compound Interest: The Eighth Wonder of the World

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world." Whether he said it or not, the sentiment captures the extraordinary power of earning returns not just on your principal, but on your accumulated gains.

## Simple vs. Compound Interest

**Simple interest** earns interest only on the original principal. $10,000 at 7% simple interest earns $700 every year.

**Compound interest** earns interest on the principal PLUS all previously accumulated interest. $10,000 at 7% compounded annually:
- Year 1: $10,700
- Year 2: $11,449 (7% on $10,700)
- Year 10: $19,672
- Year 30: $76,123

That $10,000 becomes $76,123 — more than 7x — without adding a single dollar.

## The Rule of 72

A quick mental math shortcut: divide 72 by your annual return rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money.

- 6% return → doubles every 12 years
- 8% return → doubles every 9 years  
- 10% return → doubles every 7.2 years

## Time is the Critical Variable

The most powerful lever in compounding is time. Starting early beats starting big.

**Starting at 25 vs. 35, both contributing $500/month until age 65 at 7% annual return:**
- Age 25 start: ~$1,310,000
- Age 35 start: ~$610,000

The 10-year head start roughly doubles the final amount, even though the 35-year-old only missed 10 years of contributions.

## Compounding Frequency

The more frequently interest compounds, the faster your money grows:
- Annual compounding: $10,000 at 10% = $11,000 after year 1
- Monthly compounding: $10,000 at 10% ≈ $11,047 after year 1
- Daily compounding: $10,000 at 10% ≈ $11,052 after year 1

For long-term stock investing, the difference between compounding frequencies matters less than the annual return rate and time horizon.

## Negative Compounding

Compound interest works against you with debt. Credit card debt at 20% APR compounds monthly, rapidly snowballing. This is why high-interest debt should be paid off before investing — the guaranteed 20% "return" of eliminating the debt beats most investments.
