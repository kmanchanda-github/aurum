# ETFs Explained: Exchange-Traded Funds

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) are investment funds that trade on stock exchanges, just like individual stocks. They hold a collection of assets — stocks, bonds, commodities — and allow investors to buy exposure to many securities in a single transaction.

## ETF vs. Mutual Fund

Both pool investor money to buy a collection of securities. Key differences:

| Feature | ETF | Mutual Fund |
|---|---|---|
| Trading | Throughout the day at market price | Once per day at NAV |
| Minimum investment | 1 share (or fractional) | Often $500–$3,000 |
| Tax efficiency | High (in-kind redemptions) | Lower |
| Expense ratio | Lower on average | Varies (index funds similar) |

## How ETFs Work

When you buy an ETF, you're buying shares in a fund that holds underlying assets. Authorized participants (large financial institutions) create and redeem ETF shares in large blocks by exchanging a basket of the underlying securities. This mechanism keeps ETF prices close to the net asset value of the holdings.

The creation/redemption mechanism also makes ETFs more tax-efficient than mutual funds. Capital gains distributions, common in actively managed mutual funds, are rare in ETFs.

## Types of ETFs

**Equity ETFs:** Track stock indexes (SPY, QQQ, VTI), sectors (XLK for tech, XLE for energy), or factors (value, growth, momentum).

**Bond ETFs:** Track government (TLT, IEF), corporate (LQD, HYG), or municipal bond indexes. Provide daily liquidity unlike individual bonds.

**Commodity ETFs:** Track gold (GLD, IAU), oil, or broad commodity indexes. Note: some commodity ETFs use futures and may have tracking issues.

**International ETFs:** Developed markets (EFA, VEA) or emerging markets (EEM, VWO). Easy way to add geographic diversification.

**Thematic ETFs:** Focus on trends like clean energy, cybersecurity, robotics. Higher fees and more concentrated risk.

## Selecting an ETF

1. **Expense ratio:** Lower is better. Broad index ETFs range from 0.03% to 0.20%.
2. **Assets under management (AUM):** Larger funds have better liquidity and are unlikely to close.
3. **Bid-ask spread:** Tight spreads (< 0.05%) indicate good liquidity.
4. **Tracking error:** How closely the ETF follows its index.

## ETFs in a Portfolio

A complete, low-cost portfolio can be built with just 3–4 ETFs:
1. US total stock market (VTI or SCHB)
2. International stocks (VXUS or SCHE)
3. US bonds (BND or AGG)

This "three-fund portfolio" provides diversification across thousands of securities at minimal cost.
