# Options Basics for Investors

Options are financial contracts that give the buyer the right — but not the obligation — to buy or sell a stock at a specific price before a specific date. They're more complex than stocks but understanding the basics helps investors recognize both opportunities and risks.

## The Two Types of Options

**Call Option**: Gives you the right to *buy* a stock at a set price (the "strike price") before the expiration date.
- You buy a call when you think the stock will rise.
- Max loss: the premium you paid.
- Max gain: theoretically unlimited.

**Put Option**: Gives you the right to *sell* a stock at the strike price before expiration.
- You buy a put when you think the stock will fall, or to protect a stock you own.
- Max loss: the premium paid.
- Max gain: the strike price minus zero (if the stock goes to $0).

## Key Terms

**Premium**: The price you pay to buy an option contract. One contract = 100 shares.

**Strike Price**: The price at which the option allows you to buy (call) or sell (put).

**Expiration Date**: Options expire worthless if not exercised before this date.

**In the Money (ITM)**: A call is ITM when the stock price is above the strike; a put is ITM when below.

**Out of the Money (OTM)**: The option has no intrinsic value but may still have time value.

## A Simple Example

You think Apple ($175) will rise. You buy 1 call option with a $180 strike expiring in 60 days for a $3 premium. Total cost: $300 (3 × 100 shares).

- If Apple rises to $195 before expiration: your call is worth at least $15 ($1,500 total). Profit: $1,200.
- If Apple stays at $172: the call expires worthless. You lose your $300 premium.

## Conservative Uses of Options

Most beginner-friendly options strategies involve using options to generate income or protect existing positions — not speculation:

**Covered Call**: Own 100 shares of a stock; sell a call option against it. You collect the premium and agree to sell at the strike price if reached. Generates income but caps your upside.

**Protective Put**: Own 100 shares; buy a put option as insurance. If the stock falls sharply, the put limits your loss. Like buying insurance on your portfolio.

## Risks

Options are **time-sensitive** — they lose value every day they exist (called "time decay" or theta). Most beginners who buy options out of the money lose money because the stock doesn't move enough, fast enough.

Selling naked options (without owning the underlying stock) carries theoretically unlimited risk and should only be done by experienced investors.

## Who Options Are For

Options are best suited for investors who:
- Already understand stocks well
- Have a specific view on price, timing, or volatility
- Want to hedge (reduce risk) on existing positions
- Want to generate income on holdings they plan to keep

For most beginners, sticking to stocks and index funds is the smarter path before adding the complexity of options.
