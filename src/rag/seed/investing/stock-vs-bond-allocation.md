# Stock vs. Bond Allocation

How you split your portfolio between stocks and bonds is the single most important decision in investing. This allocation determines roughly 90% of your long-term returns and volatility — more than any individual security selection.

## Why Stocks and Bonds Behave Differently

**Stocks** represent ownership in businesses. They grow with company earnings and the economy but are volatile — capable of falling 30–50% in a bad year and recovering over time.

**Bonds** are loans to governments or corporations. They provide predictable interest income and tend to hold their value (or rise) when stocks fall. But they offer lower long-term returns.

The combination dampens portfolio swings without eliminating growth.

## Classic Allocation Rules

**The 60/40 Portfolio**: 60% stocks, 40% bonds. A classic "balanced" allocation used by pension funds and moderate investors for decades.

**The Age Rule**: Own your age in bonds. A 30-year-old holds 30% bonds, 70% stocks. A 60-year-old holds 60% bonds, 40% stocks. Simple but outdated now that people live longer.

**The 110 Rule**: 110 minus your age = stock allocation. Accounts for longer lifespans. A 40-year-old would hold 70% stocks.

**Target-Date Funds**: Automatically shift from stocks to bonds as you approach your target retirement year (e.g., a 2055 fund holds ~90% stocks today, gradually shifting to ~50% stocks by 2055).

## Allocation by Risk Tolerance and Time Horizon

| Profile | Stocks | Bonds | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aggressive (20+ yr horizon) | 90–100% | 0–10% | Young investors, high risk tolerance |
| Moderate (10–20 yr horizon) | 60–80% | 20–40% | Pre-retirement, balanced |
| Conservative (under 10 yr) | 30–50% | 50–70% | Near retirement, capital preservation |
| Income-focused | 20–40% | 60–80% | Retirees who need cash flow |

## Historical Return Tradeoffs

Over the past 50 years (approximate averages):
- 100% stocks: ~10% annual return, but max drawdown of ~50%
- 60/40: ~8% annual return, max drawdown of ~30%
- 100% bonds: ~5% annual return, max drawdown of ~15%

Adding 40% bonds to a stock portfolio reduced returns by ~2% but cut the worst crashes nearly in half.

## Rebalancing Your Allocation

As stocks rise and bonds lag, your allocation drifts. A portfolio starting at 60/40 might become 75/25 after a strong bull market, exposing you to more risk than intended. Rebalancing (selling some stocks, buying bonds) brings it back to target annually or when it drifts more than 5%.

## Beyond Stocks and Bonds

Modern portfolios sometimes add:
- **Real estate (REITs)**: 5–10% for income and inflation protection
- **International stocks**: 20–30% of the stock portion for geographic diversification
- **Cash/Short-term bonds**: For near-term spending needs or emergency fund

## Practical Starting Point

Most beginners do well starting with a target-date fund, which handles the stock/bond allocation automatically and rebalances over time. As you learn more, you can customize.
