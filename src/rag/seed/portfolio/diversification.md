# Portfolio Diversification

Diversification is the practice of spreading investments across different assets to reduce risk. The classic adage "don't put all your eggs in one basket" captures the core idea.

## Why Diversification Works

Different assets often move independently or even in opposite directions. When one investment falls, others may rise or hold steady. This reduces the overall volatility of your portfolio without necessarily sacrificing returns.

A portfolio of 30 uncorrelated stocks has far less company-specific risk than holding just 1 stock, even if the expected return is similar.

## Dimensions of Diversification

**Asset Class Diversification**
Mix stocks, bonds, real estate, and cash. When stocks fall, bonds often rise (though not always).

**Geographic Diversification**
Invest in domestic AND international markets. US stocks represent ~60% of global market cap; the other 40% provides exposure to different economies.

**Sector Diversification**
Spread across technology, healthcare, finance, consumer goods, energy, etc. Sector-specific downturns don't devastate the whole portfolio.

**Market Cap Diversification**
Include large-cap (stable), mid-cap (growth + stability), and small-cap (higher growth potential) companies.

**Time Diversification (Dollar-Cost Averaging)**
Invest regularly over time rather than a lump sum. This averages out your purchase price across market cycles.

## Common Portfolio Templates

**Three-Fund Portfolio (Bogleheads)**:
- US Total Stock Market Index Fund (~60%)
- International Stock Market Index Fund (~30%)
- US Bond Market Index Fund (~10%)

Simple, low-cost, highly diversified.

**60/40 Portfolio**: 60% stocks, 40% bonds. Classic balanced approach.

## What Diversification Can't Protect Against

Systemic market events (like the 2008 financial crisis or COVID crash) affect all asset classes simultaneously. Diversification reduces company-specific and sector-specific risk, not broad market risk.

## Measuring Diversification

**Correlation**: Assets with correlation close to -1 move opposite to each other (ideal). Assets with correlation close to +1 move together (little diversification benefit).

**Standard Deviation**: Lower portfolio standard deviation = better diversification for a given return level.
