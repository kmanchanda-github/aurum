# International Diversification

Many US investors hold almost entirely US stocks — a phenomenon called "home country bias." While the US market has been exceptional, a globally diversified portfolio reduces risk and captures growth from the world's other major economies.

## The Global Market Opportunity

The US stock market represents approximately 60% of global market capitalization. The other 40% spans:
- **Developed markets**: Europe, Japan, Australia, Canada, UK (about 25–30% of global market cap)
- **Emerging markets**: China, India, Brazil, South Korea, Taiwan, etc. (about 10–15%)

Investing only in the US means ignoring 40% of the world's investable assets.

## Why Diversify Internationally?

**Valuation differences**: US stocks frequently trade at premium valuations. International stocks, particularly in Europe and emerging markets, often trade at significant discounts. A global approach lets you buy more cheaply valued businesses.

**Economic cycle diversification**: Not all economies peak and trough simultaneously. When the US slows, other regions may be accelerating.

**Currency diversification**: Holding assets denominated in foreign currencies provides a hedge against long-term US dollar weakness.

**Historical evidence**: From 1970–2008, international stocks outperformed US stocks. US stocks dominated from 2010–2021. Leadership rotates; diversification captures both.

## Historical Return Comparison (Approximate)

| Period | US Stocks | International Stocks |
|---|---|---|
| 1970–1989 | 11.5% | 16.5% |
| 1990–2009 | 8.0% | 5.5% |
| 2010–2021 | 16% | 8% |
| Long-run (~50 yr) | ~10% | ~9% |

## Risks of International Investing

**Currency risk**: Foreign currency fluctuations affect returns for US investors. When the dollar strengthens, international returns (in dollar terms) suffer.

**Political risk**: Emerging markets carry risks of government policy changes, capital controls, or geopolitical instability (e.g., Russia's market closure in 2022).

**Different accounting standards**: Financial statements in some countries are less transparent than US GAAP standards.

**Liquidity risk**: Some international markets are less liquid; trading can be more expensive.

## How to Invest Internationally

**Simplest — total international ETF:**
- VXUS (Vanguard Total International Stock ETF) — all non-US developed + emerging markets
- IXUS (iShares Core MSCI Total International Stock ETF)

**Split developed and emerging:**
- EFA or VEA — developed markets (Europe, Asia, Australia)
- EEM or VWO — emerging markets (higher growth potential, higher risk)

**Regional:**
- EWJ — Japan only
- EWG — Germany only
- MCHI — China large caps

## How Much to Allocate?

**Common guidance:**
- Market-weight: ~40% of stocks in international (matches global market cap)
- Moderate: 20–30% of stocks in international
- Minimal: 10% international for those with high US conviction

Jack Bogle (Vanguard founder) famously suggested 0–20% international for US investors, arguing that US multinationals already provide global exposure. Many target-date funds use 30–40% international.

## The Bottom Line

International diversification lowers portfolio volatility and reduces dependence on a single country's economy. Even a modest 20% international allocation meaningfully improves diversification. The right amount depends on your conviction, risk tolerance, and time horizon.
