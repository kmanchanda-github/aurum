# Asset Allocation: Building Your Investment Mix

Asset allocation is how you divide your investments among different asset classes — stocks, bonds, cash, real estate, and alternatives. It's the single most important decision in investing, responsible for roughly 90% of a portfolio's long-term performance variation.

## The Major Asset Classes

**Stocks (equities)** represent ownership in companies. They offer higher long-term returns (historically ~10% annually for US stocks) but with significant short-term volatility. A diversified stock portfolio can drop 30–50% in a bear market.

**Bonds (fixed income)** are loans to governments or corporations that pay regular interest. Lower returns (~3–5% historically) but more stable, and they often rise when stocks fall, providing a cushion.

**Cash and cash equivalents** (savings accounts, money market funds, CDs) offer stability and liquidity with minimal return. Essential for emergency funds; too much is a drag on long-term growth.

**Real estate** via REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts) provides exposure to property markets with stock-like liquidity. Often behaves differently from stocks and bonds.

## Classic Allocation Guidelines

The old "100 minus your age" rule: subtract your age from 100 to get your stock percentage. A 30-year-old would hold 70% stocks, 30% bonds.

Modern versions use "110 minus age" or "120 minus age" to account for longer lifespans.

**Common allocations by risk tolerance:**
| Risk Profile | Stocks | Bonds | Cash |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aggressive | 90% | 8% | 2% |
| Moderate | 70% | 25% | 5% |
| Conservative | 40% | 50% | 10% |

## Factors That Should Influence Your Allocation

**Time horizon:** The longer until you need the money, the more volatility you can afford. A 25-year-old saving for retirement can take a 50% stock market drop in stride — they have 40 years to recover.

**Risk tolerance:** How would you react if your portfolio dropped 30%? If you'd sell everything, a more conservative allocation prevents that mistake.

**Income stability:** A stable government job with a pension allows more investment risk than freelance income with no safety net.

**Goals:** Down payment in 3 years = mostly cash/bonds. Retirement in 30 years = mostly stocks.

## Lifecycle Allocation

Most target-date retirement funds (e.g., "Target 2055") automatically shift from aggressive to conservative as the target date approaches. This is a sensible, low-maintenance approach.

The key insight: you can't afford a 50% portfolio drop when you're 5 years from retirement and living off your savings. Gradually reducing stock exposure as you approach a goal is prudent risk management.
