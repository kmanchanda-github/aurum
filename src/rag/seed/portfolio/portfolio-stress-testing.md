# Portfolio Stress Testing

Stress testing a portfolio means simulating how it would perform under severe adverse conditions — market crashes, interest rate spikes, or economic recessions. It's a proactive risk management technique that helps you understand what you're truly exposed to before a crisis arrives.

## Why Stress Test?

Most investors think about returns during normal or good markets. Stress testing forces you to answer: "How much can I afford to lose, and will I stay invested if that happens?" If the answer reveals you'd panic-sell at a 40% loss, you need a more conservative allocation before the crash — not during it.

## Common Stress Scenarios

**Historical scenarios** — replay actual crises:

| Scenario | S&P 500 Peak-to-Trough | Bonds (10-yr Treasury) |
|---|---|---|
| 2008 Financial Crisis | -57% | +26% |
| 2020 COVID Crash | -34% | +19% |
| 2000–2002 Dot-Com Bust | -49% | +30% |
| 1973–1974 Oil Crisis | -48% | Flat |
| 2022 Rate Shock | -25% | -18% (unusual: stocks AND bonds fell) |

For each scenario, calculate what your current portfolio would have lost.

**Hypothetical scenarios** — test specific risks:
- Interest rates rise 3% suddenly (damages bonds and rate-sensitive stocks)
- Inflation spikes to 8% (hurts bonds, mixed for stocks, good for REITs and commodities)
- A single sector collapses (e.g., tech falls 60% — how much of your portfolio is tech?)
- Dollar strengthens 20% (hurts international stock returns)

## How to Stress Test Your Portfolio

**Step 1**: List all holdings with current market values and percentage allocation.

**Step 2**: Find beta for each holding (available on Yahoo Finance, Morningstar).

**Step 3**: Apply the stress scenario return to each holding using beta as a scaling factor.
- If scenario: S&P -40%, holding with beta 1.5 → estimated loss: -60%
- Bonds in 2008 scenario: +26%

**Step 4**: Sum the weighted losses/gains across the portfolio.

**Step 5**: Compare the simulated portfolio value to your financial needs:
- Could you cover living expenses for 12 months?
- Would you need to sell investments in the trough to pay bills?
- Would you emotionally be able to stay invested?

## Using Online Tools

Several free tools help with stress testing:
- **Portfolio Visualizer** (portfoliovisualizer.com): Backtest any allocation against historical periods
- **Morningstar Portfolio Manager**: Scenario analysis on funds
- **Your brokerage**: Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard offer basic stress test tools

## Practical Takeaways

**If your 2008 simulation shows a 50% loss and you'd need the money in 5 years**: You're over-allocated to stocks.

**If your 2022 simulation shows you lost big on both stocks AND bonds**: Consider adding real assets (commodities, TIPS) as an inflation hedge.

**If your tech allocation is 40%**: A sector-specific crash would be devastating — consider trimming.

Stress testing isn't about predicting the future. It's about understanding your exposure so you can make rational decisions now, before fear drives you to make irrational ones later.
