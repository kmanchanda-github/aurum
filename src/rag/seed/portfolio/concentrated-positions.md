# Managing Concentrated Stock Positions

A concentrated position is when a single stock represents a large percentage — typically 10% or more — of your total investment portfolio. This often happens with company stock from employment (RSUs, stock options, ESOP), an inheritance, or a single investment that has grown dramatically. Managing it well is one of the most valuable things you can do for your financial future.

## Why Concentration Is Risky

Diversification reduces company-specific risk. With a concentrated position, your financial wellbeing is tied to one company's fate. Even great companies can:
- Miss earnings and fall 40% in a day (Meta in 2022: -26% in one session)
- Face regulatory action, accounting fraud, or industry disruption
- Underperform the market for a decade (even GE, once the world's most valuable company)

**The math**: A stock needs to rise 100% to recover from a 50% loss. Concentrated positions carry this risk in full.

## Strategies to Reduce Concentration

### 1. Staged Selling
Sell a fixed percentage of the position over time — e.g., 10% per quarter over 2–3 years. This spreads out the capital gains tax bill and reduces timing risk (you're not selling everything at a market peak or trough).

### 2. Tax-Loss Harvesting Pairs
In years when other investments have losses, harvest those losses to offset gains from selling the concentrated position. This reduces the tax cost of diversifying.

### 3. Charitable Giving (Donor-Advised Fund)
Donate appreciated shares directly to a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) rather than selling. You get a full fair-market-value deduction, pay zero capital gains tax, and can grant to charities over time.

**Example**: Own $50,000 of stock with $10,000 cost basis. If you sell and donate cash: pay $6,000+ in capital gains tax first. If you donate the shares directly: no capital gains tax, full $50,000 deduction.

### 4. Qualified Opportunity Zone Funds
Rolling gains from selling a concentrated position into a Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) fund can defer taxes and reduce them if held long enough. Complex and illiquid — consult a tax advisor.

### 5. Exchange Funds
Pool your concentrated stock with other investors holding different concentrated positions. You exchange your shares for a share of the diversified fund. No immediate tax. Only available to accredited investors, typically with a 7-year lock-up.

### 6. Protective Puts or Collars (Options)
Buy put options to insure against a large decline, or use a collar (buy puts + sell calls) to define a range. Expensive but provides downside protection while you devise a longer-term plan.

## The Emotional Challenge

Concentrated positions often carry emotional weight — company loyalty, belief in the CEO, or fear of admitting the position is too large. Research consistently shows investors hold concentrated positions too long. The rational framework: would you buy this much of this single stock today? If not, it's time to rebalance.

## Tax-Smart Execution

Work with a fee-only CPA or financial advisor when unwinding large positions. The tax implications (long-term vs. short-term gains, AMT for ISO stock options, NIIT) can be significant, and the right execution sequence matters.

## Employee Stock: Special Considerations

- **RSUs**: Taxed as ordinary income when they vest; subsequent gain/loss is capital gain. Sell promptly after vesting unless you have strong conviction.
- **ISOs**: Subject to AMT rules. Timing of exercise and sale is complex.
- **ESPP shares**: Often discounted 15%; many advisors suggest selling immediately after purchase to lock in the guaranteed gain.
