# Portfolio Rebalancing: Keeping Your Allocation on Track

Over time, different assets grow at different rates, causing your portfolio to drift from its target allocation. Rebalancing is the process of restoring your portfolio to its intended mix.

## Why Rebalancing Matters

Suppose you start with 70% stocks / 30% bonds. After a strong bull market, stocks might grow to 85% of your portfolio. Now you're taking more risk than intended. If a bear market hits, the bigger stock position means larger losses than your plan anticipated.

Rebalancing systematically enforces "buy low, sell high" — you trim what's grown expensive and add to what's lagged.

## Rebalancing Strategies

**Calendar rebalancing:** Review and rebalance on a fixed schedule — quarterly, semi-annually, or annually. Simple and predictable. Annual rebalancing works well for most investors.

**Threshold rebalancing:** Rebalance whenever an asset class drifts more than 5% from its target. A 70/30 portfolio becomes 75/25 → trigger rebalancing. More precise but requires monitoring.

**Hybrid approach:** Check annually, but only rebalance if drift exceeds 5%. Balances simplicity with precision.

## The Tax Implications

In taxable accounts, selling appreciated assets to rebalance triggers capital gains taxes. Strategies to minimize the tax drag:

**Use new contributions:** Direct new money into underweight asset classes instead of selling overweight ones.

**Rebalance in tax-advantaged accounts:** 401(k)s and IRAs have no capital gains tax. Do your rebalancing here first.

**Tax-loss harvesting:** When rebalancing involves selling losers, you can harvest those losses to offset gains elsewhere.

## How Often Should You Rebalance?

Research suggests annual rebalancing captures most of the benefit with minimal transaction costs. More frequent rebalancing (monthly/quarterly) adds transaction costs without significantly improving returns.

For most investors, the simplest approach works: once a year, check your allocation and rebalance if you've drifted more than 5–10% from your targets.

## Rebalancing in Retirement

In retirement, rebalancing takes on additional importance because sequence-of-returns risk is highest early in retirement. Maintaining your target allocation prevents both excessive risk and insufficient growth.
