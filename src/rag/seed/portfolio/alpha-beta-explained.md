# Alpha and Beta Explained

Alpha and beta are two of the most commonly used metrics in portfolio analysis. Both measure your investment's relationship to the broader market, but they answer different questions: beta measures sensitivity to market swings, while alpha measures whether you're getting rewarded above what that risk would predict.

## Beta: Market Sensitivity

Beta measures how much a stock or portfolio moves relative to a benchmark (usually the S&P 500).

| Beta | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1.0 | Moves in lockstep with the market |
| > 1.0 | More volatile than the market (amplifies moves) |
| < 1.0 | Less volatile than the market (dampens moves) |
| 0 | No correlation to market (e.g., cash) |
| Negative | Moves opposite to market (e.g., some gold funds, inverse ETFs) |

**Example**: A stock with beta = 1.4 tends to rise 14% when the market rises 10%, and fall 14% when the market falls 10%.

**High-beta examples**: Small growth tech stocks, leveraged ETFs
**Low-beta examples**: Utility stocks, consumer staples, gold

## Alpha: Excess Return

Alpha measures how much a portfolio returned above (or below) what its beta exposure would predict.

**Formula**: Alpha = Actual Return − (Risk-Free Rate + Beta × (Market Return − Risk-Free Rate))

**Example**:
- Market returned 12%, risk-free rate = 5%, portfolio beta = 1.0
- Expected return = 5% + 1.0 × (12% − 5%) = 12%
- If portfolio actually returned 15%: Alpha = +3%
- If portfolio returned 10%: Alpha = -2%

Positive alpha means the manager (or stock) added value beyond what the market exposure alone explains. Negative alpha means they underperformed their risk-adjusted expectation.

## How They Work Together

A fund with high beta and high returns doesn't necessarily have positive alpha — it may have just taken more market risk. Alpha strips out the beta effect to reveal true skill (or luck).

**Example comparison**:
- Fund A: 18% return, beta = 1.8 → Expected return given beta = 17.6% → Alpha = +0.4%
- Fund B: 14% return, beta = 0.9 → Expected return = 11.3% → Alpha = +2.7%

Fund B generated significantly more alpha despite a lower raw return.

## Portfolio-Level Beta

You can calculate the beta of your entire portfolio by weighting individual stock betas:
- $10K in AAPL (beta 1.2) + $10K in JNJ (beta 0.55) = portfolio beta of ~0.88

Knowing your portfolio beta helps you understand how much you'd expect to lose in a market crash.

## Limitations

**Beta is backward-looking**: A stock's historical beta may change as the company evolves.

**Alpha is often luck**: Studies show most mutual fund managers don't generate persistent positive alpha after fees. The few who do may be exploiting specific market inefficiencies that close over time.

**Benchmark matters**: Alpha and beta depend on which benchmark you use. A bond fund compared to the S&P 500 will look very different than one compared to the Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index.

## Practical Use

- Use **beta** to understand your portfolio's market sensitivity and potential downside in a crash
- Use **alpha** to evaluate fund managers — if a fund charges 1% annually but generates only 0.5% alpha, you're net negative
- For passive investors, target beta ≈ 1 (full market exposure) and don't worry about alpha — just minimize fees
