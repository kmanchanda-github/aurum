# Inflation and Investing

Inflation — the gradual rise in prices over time — is one of the most important forces affecting your investments. Understanding how inflation interacts with different asset classes helps you build a portfolio that preserves purchasing power over decades.

## What Inflation Does to Your Money

If inflation runs at 3% per year, $100 today buys what $74 buys in 10 years. Cash sitting in a savings account earning 1% loses real purchasing power every year. Investing is fundamentally about outpacing inflation.

**Real return = Nominal return − Inflation rate**

If your portfolio returns 7% per year and inflation is 3%, your real return is 4%. That's what actually matters for building wealth.

## Historical Inflation Context

- US average inflation 1960–2023: ~3.7% per year
- 1970s peak: 14.6% (1980)
- 2022 spike: 9.1% (highest since 1981)
- 2024: ~3.0%, trending toward Fed's 2% target

## How Different Assets Respond to Inflation

**Stocks — mixed**
Over long periods (10+ years), stocks historically beat inflation. Companies can raise prices, grow earnings, and their real assets appreciate. However, in the short run, high inflation often causes stocks to fall as interest rates rise and future earnings get discounted more heavily.

**Bonds — hurt by inflation**
Inflation is a bond investor's worst enemy. Fixed interest payments lose purchasing power when prices rise. The 2022 bond crash (-18%) was driven by surging inflation and rate hikes. Long-duration bonds are most vulnerable.

**TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) — designed for inflation**
TIPS principal adjusts with the CPI. If inflation runs 6%, your TIPS principal grows 6%. The real yield is guaranteed. Best held in tax-advantaged accounts (the inflation adjustment is taxed annually).

**Real Estate / REITs — generally good hedge**
Property values and rents tend to rise with inflation. Many REIT leases include rent escalators tied to CPI.

**Commodities — strong inflation hedge**
Oil, gold, agricultural products, and metals tend to rise with inflation — sometimes dramatically. However, commodities are volatile and produce no income.

**Gold — inconsistent**
Gold is often called an inflation hedge, but evidence is mixed over shorter periods. It performs well in crisis/panic but may not keep pace with inflation over a decade.

**I-Bonds — excellent for individuals**
US Series I savings bonds earn a composite rate tied to CPI. As of 2024, individuals can purchase up to $10,000/year. The inflation-adjusted yield is guaranteed. Held for 5+ years, they're nearly ideal for emergency fund overflow.

## Inflation-Resilient Portfolio Strategies

1. **Tilt toward shorter-duration bonds** (less interest rate sensitivity)
2. **Add TIPS or I-Bonds** for guaranteed inflation protection
3. **Include REITs** for real asset exposure
4. **Maintain stock allocation**: equities are the best long-run inflation hedge
5. **Reduce cash drag**: don't hold more cash than needed for near-term expenses

## The Fed's Role

The Federal Reserve targets 2% annual inflation. When inflation runs high, the Fed raises the federal funds rate, which ripples through the economy by making borrowing more expensive, slowing spending, and eventually reducing price pressures. Higher rates hurt bonds and growth stocks in the near term but are ultimately good for long-term investors because they restore real yields.

## Bottom Line

Inflation makes investing necessary, not optional. A well-diversified portfolio of stocks, TIPS, and real assets outpaces inflation over long periods. Cash and long-duration bonds are most vulnerable.
