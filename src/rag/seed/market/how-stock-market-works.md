# How the Stock Market Works

The stock market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers trade ownership shares (stock) in publicly listed companies. Understanding how it operates helps you interpret market movements and make informed decisions.

## What Is a Stock?

A stock (or share) represents a fractional ownership interest in a company. If Apple has 15 billion shares outstanding and you own 100 shares, you own 100/15,000,000,000 of Apple.

As a shareholder, you may receive:
- **Dividends**: A share of company profits distributed to shareholders
- **Capital appreciation**: Your shares become worth more as the company grows
- **Voting rights**: Ability to vote on certain corporate matters (common stock)

## Major US Exchanges

**NYSE (New York Stock Exchange)**: Oldest and largest by market capitalization. Home to many large, established companies.

**NASDAQ**: Technology-focused exchange. Listed companies include Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google.

**CBOE**: Primarily derivatives (options and futures).

## Key Market Indices

Indices track the performance of a basket of stocks, representing the broader market:

- **S&P 500 (^GSPC)**: 500 largest US companies. The most widely followed benchmark.
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average (^DJI)**: 30 major US companies. Oldest index, price-weighted.
- **NASDAQ Composite (^IXIC)**: All NASDAQ-listed stocks (~3,000 companies).
- **Russell 2000**: 2,000 small-cap US companies.

## Market Hours

US stock markets trade Monday-Friday, 9:30 AM - 4:00 PM Eastern Time. Pre-market and after-hours trading occur with lower liquidity.

## What Moves Stock Prices?

**Company-specific factors**:
- Earnings reports (quarterly results vs. expectations)
- Management changes
- Product launches or failures
- Acquisitions and mergers

**Macro factors**:
- Interest rates (higher rates generally hurt stocks)
- Inflation data
- Economic indicators (GDP, unemployment)
- Federal Reserve policy

**Market sentiment**: Fear and greed drive short-term movements. The VIX (Volatility Index) measures expected market volatility — higher VIX = more fear.

## Bull vs. Bear Markets

**Bull market**: Sustained rise of 20%+ from recent lows. Associated with economic growth and investor optimism.

**Bear market**: Decline of 20%+ from recent highs. Associated with economic slowdown or recession fears.

Historically, bull markets last much longer than bear markets. The average bull market lasts ~5 years; the average bear market ~10 months.
