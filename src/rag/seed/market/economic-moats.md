# Economic Moats Explained

An economic moat is a sustainable competitive advantage that allows a company to defend its market position and earn above-average profits for years or decades. The term was popularized by Warren Buffett, who looks for businesses with "wide moats" as long-term investments.

## Why Moats Matter

Competition is the enemy of profits. Without a moat, any company earning high profits will attract competitors who undercut prices until margins return to normal. A moat is what makes a business durable — the reason a company can stay profitable despite competition.

## The Five Types of Economic Moats

### 1. Network Effects
A product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Each new user adds value for all existing users.

**Examples**: Visa/Mastercard (more merchants accept it → more cardholders → more merchants), social networks (Facebook, LinkedIn), marketplaces (eBay, Airbnb).

**Why it's powerful**: Once a network reaches critical mass, it's nearly impossible for a new entrant to compete because they start with zero users.

### 2. Cost Advantages
The company can produce its product at lower cost than competitors through scale, proprietary processes, or geography.

**Examples**: Walmart (scale → supplier pricing power), Amazon (fulfillment infrastructure), GEICO (direct-to-consumer model cuts out agents).

**Why it's powerful**: Either keeps prices lower (volume advantage) or earns higher margins at the same price.

### 3. Switching Costs
Customers become "locked in" because changing to a competitor is expensive, time-consuming, or risky.

**Examples**: Salesforce (CRM deeply integrated into company workflows), Oracle databases, Adobe Creative Suite, Enterprise software generally.

**Why it's powerful**: Customers tolerate price increases because switching costs are higher than the price increase.

### 4. Intangible Assets
Brands, patents, licenses, or regulatory approvals that competitors can't easily replicate.

**Brand moat examples**: Apple, Coca-Cola, Nike, LVMH (luxury brands)
**Patent moat examples**: Pharmaceutical companies (new drugs protected for 20 years)
**Regulatory moat examples**: Utilities (legal monopolies), ratings agencies (SEC-licensed)

**Why it's powerful**: Competitors can't legally copy patented products; consumers pay premiums for trusted brands.

### 5. Efficient Scale
A market is only large enough to profitably support one or a few competitors. Entering would destroy profits for everyone.

**Examples**: Pipelines, regional airports, local utility companies, railroad networks.

**Why it's powerful**: A rational competitor won't enter a market where entry makes the market unprofitable for all participants.

## Moat Durability and Erosion

Not all moats are permanent. Moats can be eroded by:
- **Technological disruption** (digital cameras destroyed Kodak's brand and patent moat)
- **Regulatory changes** (pharmaceutical patents expire; generic drugs enter)
- **Shifting consumer preferences** (department store brands lost to e-commerce)
- **Competitor innovation** (Google's network effects in search face pressure from AI-powered search)

This is why Buffett also looks for companies run by capable, honest management — moats need to be maintained and extended over time.

## How to Identify Moats

**Look at returns on invested capital (ROIC)**: Companies with moats consistently earn ROIC above their cost of capital (typically above 12–15% for a decade or more).

**Pricing power test**: Can the company raise prices without losing significant market share? If yes, there's a moat.

**Gross margin trend**: Companies with moats tend to have stable or improving gross margins even as competitors try to undercut them.

## Moat Width: Narrow vs. Wide

Morningstar classifies moats as Wide, Narrow, or None:
- **Wide moat**: Competitive advantage expected to last 20+ years
- **Narrow moat**: Advantage expected to last 10+ years but is more vulnerable
- **No moat**: Faces intense competition with no structural advantages

Wide-moat companies have historically outperformed the market over long periods with lower volatility.
