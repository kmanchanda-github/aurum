# Key Market Indicators Explained

Financial markets generate a constant stream of indicators and signals. Knowing which ones are genuinely useful — and how to interpret them — helps you understand market conditions without being overwhelmed by noise.

## Volatility: The VIX

The **VIX (CBOE Volatility Index)** measures the market's expectation of 30-day volatility in the S&P 500, derived from options prices. It's often called the "fear gauge."

| VIX Level | Market Sentiment |
|---|---|
| Under 15 | Complacency, low fear |
| 15–25 | Normal market conditions |
| 25–35 | Elevated concern |
| Above 35 | High fear, potential panic |
| Above 50 | Crisis conditions (2008, March 2020) |

**Contrarian signal**: Extremely high VIX readings (40+) often coincide with market bottoms. When fear peaks, selling pressure exhausts itself.

## Valuation: P/E Ratio and CAPE

**Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio**: Stock price ÷ annual earnings per share.
- S&P 500 historical average P/E: ~16
- Above 25: expensive relative to history
- Below 14: cheap relative to history

**Shiller CAPE (Cyclically Adjusted P/E)**: Uses 10-year average inflation-adjusted earnings instead of a single year — smooths out business cycle swings. More predictive of long-term returns than the 1-year P/E.
- CAPE above 30 has historically predicted below-average 10-year returns
- CAPE below 15 has historically predicted above-average 10-year returns

## Breadth: Advance-Decline Line

The advance-decline (A/D) line tracks how many stocks are rising vs. falling on a given day. If the S&P 500 is rising but only a handful of large-cap stocks are driving it while most stocks are falling, market breadth is "narrow" — often a warning sign that the rally is unsustainable.

**Healthy bull market**: S&P 500 rising AND most stocks participating (broad breadth)
**Warning sign**: Index rising but A/D line flattening or falling

## Credit: High-Yield Spreads

High-yield (junk bond) spreads measure the extra yield investors demand to own risky corporate debt vs. US Treasuries. When spreads widen:
- Investors are worried about corporate credit quality and defaults
- Often precedes stock market turbulence
- Suggests economic stress or recession risk

**Spread under 3%**: Credit market healthy
**Spread 5–8%**: Elevated stress
**Spread above 10%**: Crisis conditions (2008: peaked at ~22%)

## Economic: Yield Curve

As discussed elsewhere, an inverted yield curve (2-year Treasury yield > 10-year Treasury yield) has preceded every US recession since 1955 with 12–18 month lag.

## Sentiment: Put/Call Ratio

Options traders buy calls (bullish bets) and puts (bearish bets). The put/call ratio measures relative demand.
- **High ratio (> 1.2)**: More puts than calls — fear dominates, potentially contrarian bullish signal
- **Low ratio (< 0.6)**: More calls — complacency and euphoria, potentially contrarian bearish signal

## Momentum: 200-Day Moving Average

The 200-day moving average (200 DMA) of the S&P 500 is one of the most widely watched technical signals:
- **Price above 200 DMA**: Long-term uptrend
- **Price below 200 DMA**: Long-term downtrend
- **"Death cross"**: 50 DMA crosses below 200 DMA — bearish signal
- **"Golden cross"**: 50 DMA crosses above 200 DMA — bullish signal

## How to Use These Indicators

Don't use any single indicator to make binary buy/sell decisions. Combine them for a fuller picture:
- VIX very high + CAPE cheap + spreads narrowing = potentially great buying opportunity
- VIX very low + CAPE expensive + breadth narrow = elevated caution warranted

Most long-term investors check these once a month or quarter, not daily. They inform asset allocation decisions, not panic selling.
