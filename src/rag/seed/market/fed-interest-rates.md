# The Fed and Interest Rates

The Federal Reserve's interest rate decisions are the most influential single factor in US financial markets. Understanding how the Fed works and why it moves rates helps investors anticipate market-wide impacts.

## What Is the Federal Reserve?

The Federal Reserve (the "Fed") is the US central bank. Its dual mandate from Congress is:
1. **Maximum employment**: Keep unemployment as low as possible without overheating the economy
2. **Stable prices**: Target 2% annual inflation

The Fed doesn't control mortgage rates or bond yields directly, but it sets the **federal funds rate** — the overnight rate at which banks lend to each other. This rate cascades through the entire economy.

## How Rate Changes Affect Markets

**When the Fed raises rates:**
- Borrowing becomes more expensive (mortgages, car loans, credit cards, business loans)
- Bonds fall in price (new bonds offer higher yields; old ones become less attractive)
- Growth and tech stocks fall hardest (their future earnings are discounted at a higher rate)
- The US dollar typically strengthens
- Savings accounts and money market funds pay more

**When the Fed cuts rates:**
- Borrowing gets cheaper, stimulating economic activity
- Bonds rise in price
- Growth stocks and real estate tend to rally
- The dollar may weaken
- Savers earn less on cash

## The Yield Curve

The yield curve plots interest rates across different bond maturities (3-month, 2-year, 10-year, 30-year). Normally, longer maturities pay higher yields (upward slope). When short-term rates exceed long-term rates, the curve "inverts" — a reliable recession predictor historically.

**Inverted yield curve (2-year > 10-year)**: Has preceded every US recession since 1955, typically 12–18 months before the recession begins.

## Fed Meetings and Signals

The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) meets 8 times per year. After each meeting, it releases:
- A rate decision (hike, hold, or cut)
- A policy statement
- A "dot plot" showing where each Fed member expects rates to be in coming years
- A press conference from the Fed Chair

Markets often move on the *language* of the statement, not just the rate decision. "Data dependent" suggests flexibility; "resolute" signals more hikes ahead.

## Rate Cycles

The Fed moves in cycles:
- **Tightening cycle**: Series of rate hikes to cool inflation (e.g., 2022–2023: 0% → 5.25%)
- **Easing cycle**: Series of cuts to stimulate growth (e.g., 2020: 1.75% → 0% in one month)
- **Pause**: Holding rates steady while assessing data

## How Investors Should Respond

**Don't try to time the Fed**: Attempting to trade in and out of the market based on predicted Fed moves is notoriously difficult. Markets often price in rate changes months ahead.

**Adjust duration**: When rates are rising, shorter-duration bonds lose less value. When rates are expected to fall, longer-duration bonds gain more.

**Stay diversified**: A portfolio of stocks + bonds + real assets handles different rate environments differently. Diversification reduces the need to predict the Fed.

**Opportunities in high-rate environments**: High-yield savings accounts, CDs, and Treasury bills become attractive alternatives when the fed funds rate exceeds 4-5%.

## Historical Fed Funds Rate

- 1981 peak: 20% (Paul Volcker fighting inflation)
- 2008–2015: 0–0.25% (post-financial crisis stimulus)
- 2022–2023: 0% → 5.25–5.50% (fastest hiking cycle in 40 years)
- 2024: Beginning of gradual cuts back toward neutral (~3%)
