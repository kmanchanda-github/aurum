# Bonds and Interest Rates: The Inverse Relationship

Bonds are debt instruments — you lend money to a government or corporation in exchange for regular interest payments (coupon) and the return of principal at maturity. Understanding how bond prices relate to interest rates is fundamental to portfolio management.

## The Inverse Relationship

When interest rates rise, bond prices fall. When rates fall, prices rise. This inverse relationship confuses many new investors.

**Why?** Suppose you own a bond paying 3% annually. Then market rates rise to 5%. Your bond's fixed 3% is now below market rate — nobody wants it at face value. To compensate buyers, the price falls until the 3% coupon represents a 5% yield on the lower price.

**Duration** measures a bond's price sensitivity to interest rates. A bond with 10-year duration falls roughly 10% for every 1% rise in interest rates.

## Types of Bonds

**U.S. Treasury bonds:** Backed by the full faith and credit of the US government. Considered the safest investment in the world. Treasury bills (< 1 year), notes (1–10 years), and bonds (10–30 years).

**TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities):** Principal adjusts with inflation. The interest rate is real (inflation-adjusted), protecting purchasing power.

**Municipal bonds (munis):** Issued by state and local governments. Interest is exempt from federal income tax (and often state tax). Particularly valuable for high-income investors.

**Corporate bonds:** Higher yields than treasuries to compensate for default risk. Investment-grade vs. high-yield ("junk") bonds.

**Agency bonds:** Issued by government-sponsored enterprises (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac). Slightly higher yield than Treasuries.

## Yield Curve

The yield curve plots interest rates across different maturities. Normally slopes upward (longer maturities pay more). An inverted yield curve (short rates above long rates) has preceded most US recessions.

## Bonds in a Portfolio

Bonds serve three roles:
1. **Stability:** They fluctuate less than stocks
2. **Income:** Coupon payments provide regular cash flow
3. **Diversification:** Bonds often rally when stocks fall (flight to safety)

The classic 60/40 stock/bond portfolio has provided solid risk-adjusted returns for decades, though the correlation shifts during inflation-driven bear markets (as seen in 2022 when both fell).
