# IPOs Explained

An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is when a private company sells shares to the public for the first time, listing on a stock exchange. IPOs generate enormous media attention, but the reality for individual investors is more complicated than the headlines suggest.

## How an IPO Works

1. **Company hires investment banks** (underwriters): Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, etc. act as intermediaries between the company and investors.
2. **Roadshow**: Company executives travel to meet institutional investors (pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds) to gauge interest and set a price range.
3. **Pricing**: The night before the IPO, banks and the company agree on the final offering price. Institutional investors get shares at this price.
4. **First day of trading**: Shares open on the exchange. Retail (individual) investors can buy at whatever the market price is — often already above the IPO price.

## Who Gets IPO Shares at the Offering Price?

Mainly institutional investors and wealthy clients of the underwriting banks. By the time most retail investors can buy, the stock is already trading on the open market, often 20–50% above the offering price on a "hot" IPO.

**Example**: Airbnb IPO (Dec 2020): IPO price $68 → opened at $146 on the first day. Retail buyers paid 2x the price that institutional investors paid.

## IPO Performance Data

- On average, IPOs pop ~15–20% on their first day — but most of that gain goes to pre-IPO investors.
- **3-year performance**: Studies show that on average, IPOs underperform the broad market over 3–5 years. Many companies IPO at peak valuations.
- High-profile recent IPOs that fell significantly post-debut: Rivian, Snowflake (after initial pop), WeWork, Peloton, Lyft.

## Exceptions: When IPOs Do Well Long-Term

Some IPOs are genuinely great long-term investments: Amazon (1997), Google (2004), Meta (2012). But these are identifiable only in hindsight. Picking which new company will be the next Google is extremely difficult.

## Alternatives to Buying at IPO

**Wait 6 months**: IPO lockup periods prevent insiders from selling for typically 90–180 days. After lockup expiration, insider selling can pressure the stock lower, creating better entry points.

**Wait for first few earnings reports**: A company's first public earnings calls reveal how it actually performs vs. the optimistic projections in its IPO prospectus.

**Buy the sector, not the IPO**: If you believe in a new market (e.g., electric vehicles), owning a diversified ETF in that sector avoids single-company concentration.

## SPACs: An Alternative IPO Route

A Special Purpose Acquisition Company (SPAC) is a blank-check company that raises money via IPO, then merges with a private company to take it public. SPACs became popular in 2020–2021 but most SPAC mergers significantly underperformed — many fell 70–90% from peak prices.

## Reading an S-1 Prospectus

Before an IPO, the company files an S-1 with the SEC — a detailed disclosure document. Key sections to read:
- **Risk factors**: The company's own description of what could go wrong
- **Revenue and growth**: Is revenue growing? What's the growth rate trend?
- **Path to profitability**: Is the company burning cash? How long can it sustain losses?
- **Management team**: Who is running the company and what's their track record?

## Bottom Line

For most individual investors, the best IPO strategy is patient observation. Letting the hype settle, waiting for lock-ups to expire, and buying after the first few earnings reports historically produces better outcomes than chasing day-one pops.
