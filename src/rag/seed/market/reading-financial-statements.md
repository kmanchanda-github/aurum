# Reading Financial Statements: Balance Sheet, Income Statement, Cash Flow

Every public company files three core financial statements. Understanding them helps you evaluate whether a stock represents good value.

## Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

Shows revenue, expenses, and profit over a period (quarter or year).

**Key lines:**
- **Revenue (top line):** Total sales. Growth is the primary driver of stock appreciation.
- **Gross profit:** Revenue minus cost of goods sold. Gross margin = gross profit / revenue.
- **Operating income (EBIT):** Gross profit minus operating expenses (R&D, SG&A). Shows core business profitability.
- **Net income (bottom line):** After interest, taxes, one-time items. Basis for earnings per share (EPS).

**Key metrics derived from income statement:**
- P/E ratio = Stock price / EPS (earnings per share)
- Gross margin and operating margin trends

## Balance Sheet

A snapshot of what a company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at a specific date.

**Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**

- **Current assets:** Cash, accounts receivable, inventory — convert to cash within 1 year
- **Long-term assets:** Property, equipment, intangibles (patents, goodwill)
- **Current liabilities:** Accounts payable, short-term debt — due within 1 year
- **Long-term liabilities:** Bonds, long-term debt
- **Shareholders' equity (book value):** What's left for shareholders after all liabilities

**Key ratios:**
- Current ratio = Current assets / Current liabilities (> 1 is generally healthy)
- Debt-to-equity = Total debt / Equity

## Cash Flow Statement

Often considered the most important statement because it's harder to manipulate than net income. Shows actual cash moving in and out.

**Three sections:**
1. **Operating cash flow (OCF):** Cash generated from core business operations. Should be positive and growing.
2. **Investing cash flow:** Purchases/sales of assets (usually negative — spending on capex and acquisitions is good if productive).
3. **Financing cash flow:** Debt issuance/repayment, stock buybacks, dividends.

**Free cash flow (FCF) = Operating cash flow − Capital expenditures**. This is the cash available to return to shareholders or reinvest in the business. Many investors prefer to value companies on FCF rather than net income.

## Common Red Flags

- Revenue growing but operating cash flow declining (possible accounting tricks)
- Rapidly increasing accounts receivable (customers not paying)
- Inventory piling up (goods not selling)
- Debt increasing faster than equity (rising leverage)
- Net income consistently higher than operating cash flow

## Using Statements to Value Stocks

Two common approaches:
- **P/E ratio:** Compare to industry peers and historical averages
- **EV/EBITDA:** Enterprise value divided by earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization — useful for comparing companies with different capital structures
