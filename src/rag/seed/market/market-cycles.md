# Market Cycles: Bull and Bear Markets

Financial markets move in cycles — periods of rising prices followed by periods of declining prices. Understanding these cycles helps investors maintain perspective and avoid costly emotional decisions.

## Definitions

**Bull market:** A period of sustained price increases, typically defined as a 20%+ rise from a recent trough. Bull markets tend to last 3–5 years on average.

**Bear market:** A decline of 20% or more from recent highs. Bear markets are shorter on average (1–2 years) but feel longer due to the pain of watching wealth evaporate.

**Correction:** A decline of 10–20% from recent highs. Corrections occur roughly every 1–2 years and are a normal part of market behavior.

## Historical Market Cycles (S&P 500)

Since World War II, the S&P 500 has experienced roughly 14 bear markets, averaging:
- Duration: ~11 months
- Decline: -30% average, -57% at worst (2007–2009)

Bull markets have averaged:
- Duration: ~5.5 years
- Gain: +183% average

The asymmetry is clear: bear markets are shorter and less severe than bull markets are long and strong. This is why long-term investors who stay invested tend to prosper.

## The 4 Phases of a Market Cycle

**1. Accumulation (recovery):** Smart money buys after a bottom. Sentiment is pessimistic but fundamentals are improving. Media is still reporting doom.

**2. Markup (bull market):** Broad participation as economic expansion becomes visible. Optimism builds. Institutional and then retail investors pile in.

**3. Distribution (market peak):** Late-cycle exuberance. Valuations are stretched. Insiders quietly sell. Speculative excesses appear (meme stocks, crypto frenzies).

**4. Markdown (bear market):** Selling overwhelms buying. Fear dominates. Weak hands capitulate. The cycle resets.

## Why You Shouldn't Try to Time the Market

"Time in the market beats timing the market" — missing just the 10 best trading days in any 20-year period can cut your returns in half. These best days often occur during the most volatile, fearful periods when investors are tempted to be in cash.

The practical implication: stay invested through market cycles with an asset allocation you can tolerate emotionally. Rebalance systematically, don't react to headlines.

## Leading Economic Indicators

Markets lead the economy by 6–12 months. Stock prices fall before recessions begin and recover before recessions end. The market is trying to price future cash flows, not current conditions.

This means the worst point to sell is usually when the news is worst — by then, the market has already priced in much of the bad news.
