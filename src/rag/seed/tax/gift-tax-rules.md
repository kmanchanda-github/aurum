# Gift Tax Rules Explained

Giving money to family and friends is a natural part of financial planning — helping children with down payments, supporting aging parents, or passing wealth to grandchildren. The IRS has rules governing large gifts, but most people never actually owe gift tax thanks to generous exemptions.

## The Annual Gift Tax Exclusion

In 2024, you can give up to **$18,000 per person per year** to anyone without triggering any gift tax paperwork or liability. This is the annual gift tax exclusion.

**Key points**:
- The $18,000 limit applies per recipient, not per giver
- A married couple can give $36,000 to each person per year (gift splitting)
- Gifts can go to any number of people — you could give $18,000 to 20 different people ($360,000 total) with no gift tax issues
- No income tax for the recipient on gifts

**Examples**:
- Give your child $18,000 for a down payment: No gift tax
- Give your child $25,000: $7,000 counts against your lifetime exemption
- You and your spouse each give your child $18,000 = $36,000: No gift tax

## The Lifetime Gift and Estate Tax Exemption

Beyond the annual exclusion, you have a **lifetime exemption** — $13.61 million per individual in 2024 (indexed for inflation). Only gifts exceeding both the annual exclusion AND the lifetime exemption trigger actual gift tax.

**Important**: The lifetime gift and estate tax exemption is unified — gifts you make during your lifetime reduce the amount your estate can pass tax-free at death.

**Scheduled change**: The current high exemption is set to sunset after 2025. Unless Congress acts, it reverts to roughly $7 million (inflation-adjusted) in 2026. This creates planning urgency for very high net worth individuals.

## Direct Payments: The Best Strategy for Large Gifts

Certain direct payments are **completely excluded from gift tax** — they don't count against annual or lifetime limits:

- **Tuition**: Pay a school directly for someone's tuition (not room/board, books, or supplies — those don't qualify)
- **Medical expenses**: Pay a medical provider directly for someone's healthcare

**Example**: Pay your grandchild's $50,000 annual college tuition directly to the university → zero gift tax consequences, doesn't reduce any exemption.

## Gifting Appreciated Assets

Gifting appreciated stock or property instead of cash can be tax-efficient — but the recipient inherits your cost basis:

**Example**: You bought stock at $10 (cost basis). Now worth $50. You gift it to your adult child.
- Your child sells at $50 → $40 capital gain, taxed at their rate
- If your child is in the 0% capital gains bracket (income under ~$47,000 for single filers in 2024): no capital gains tax

**Contrast**: If you sold it yourself and gifted cash, you'd pay capital gains tax first.

**But**: Don't gift appreciated assets to minors who are subject to the "kiddie tax" — their unearned income above $2,500 is taxed at the parent's rate.

## Filing Requirements: Form 709

If you give anyone more than $18,000 in a year (from a single giver), you must file **IRS Form 709** (Annual Gift Tax Return). However, no tax is owed until you've exhausted your lifetime exemption. The form just tracks your running total against the $13.61M lifetime limit.

## Estate Planning Considerations

Annual gifting is an estate planning strategy: each year you give $18,000 to your children, you permanently reduce your taxable estate. A couple with 3 children could transfer $108,000/year ($36,000 × 3) estate-tax-free through annual gifting.

## 529 Plans: Superfunding

You can "front-load" a 529 college savings plan with 5 years of annual exclusion gifts at once — up to $90,000 per beneficiary ($180,000 per couple) in one year, with no gift tax. You're simply pre-using 5 years of annual exclusions. No additional annual-exclusion gifts to that beneficiary are allowed for those 5 years.

*Note: Gift and estate tax laws are complex and may change. Consult a CPA or estate planning attorney for advice specific to your situation.*
