# Self-Employment Taxes and Strategies

Freelancers, independent contractors, sole proprietors, and small business owners face a different tax reality than W-2 employees. Understanding self-employment (SE) tax, estimated payments, and deductions can save thousands of dollars per year.

## The Self-Employment Tax

When you're an employee, your employer pays half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes (FICA). As a self-employed person, you pay **both halves**:

- Social Security: 12.4% on net self-employment income up to $168,600 (2024)
- Medicare: 2.9% on all net self-employment income
- Additional Medicare: 0.9% on SE income above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married)

**Total SE tax rate**: ~15.3% on the first $168,600 of net income

**The self-employed half deduction**: You can deduct 50% of the SE tax from your gross income when calculating income tax (not from SE tax itself). This partially offsets the double burden.

**Example**: $100,000 in self-employment income:
- SE tax: $100,000 × 0.9235 × 15.3% = **$14,130** (the 0.9235 factor adjusts for the deductible portion)
- Deduction: $14,130 ÷ 2 = $7,065 reduces your adjusted gross income

## Estimated Quarterly Taxes

Self-employed people don't have withholding from paychecks. The IRS requires quarterly estimated tax payments to avoid underpayment penalties.

**Due dates**:
| Quarter | Payment Due |
|---|---|
| Jan 1 – Mar 31 | April 15 |
| Apr 1 – May 31 | June 15 |
| Jun 1 – Aug 31 | September 15 |
| Sep 1 – Dec 31 | January 15 (next year) |

**Safe harbor rule**: You avoid underpayment penalties if you pay either:
- 90% of your current year tax liability, OR
- 100% of last year's tax liability (110% if AGI > $150,000)

Most self-employed people use the prior-year safe harbor and adjust if income changes significantly.

## Key Deductions for Self-Employed Individuals

**Home Office Deduction**:
- Space used regularly and exclusively for business
- Simplified method: $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft ($1,500 max)
- Regular method: Actual expenses × (office sq ft ÷ total home sq ft)

**Health Insurance Premiums**:
- Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health, dental, and long-term care insurance premiums for themselves and family members
- This is an above-the-line deduction — reduces AGI directly

**Self-Employed Retirement Plans** (see below)

**Vehicle**:
- Standard mileage rate: 67 cents/mile for business use (2024)
- Or actual expenses × business use percentage

**Equipment and Technology**: Computers, software, cameras, tools used for business. Section 179 allows immediate expensing up to $1.16M.

**Business expenses**: Phone (business portion), internet, professional development, subscriptions, office supplies, professional fees.

## Retirement Accounts for Self-Employed

Self-employed people have access to powerful retirement vehicles with much higher contribution limits than standard IRAs:

**SEP-IRA**:
- Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income, max $69,000 (2024)
- Simple to set up and administer
- Tax-deductible contribution

**Solo 401(k)**:
- Employee contributions: up to $23,000 ($30,500 if age 50+)
- Employer contributions: up to 25% of compensation
- Total limit: $69,000 (2024)
- Can include a Roth option
- Allows loans; access to mega backdoor Roth if plan permits

**SIMPLE IRA**: For small businesses with employees; up to $16,000 employee contributions + 2-3% employer match.

**Example**: $150,000 net self-employment income:
- Solo 401k employee contribution: $23,000
- Solo 401k employer contribution: $37,500 (25% × $150,000)
- Total: $60,500 contributed tax-deferred in one year

This reduces taxable income by $60,500, saving thousands in income tax.

## LLC and S-Corp Considerations

High-earning self-employed individuals often elect S-Corp status or form an LLC taxed as an S-Corp. With an S-Corp:
- You pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to SE tax)
- Additional profits pass through as distributions (not subject to SE tax)

**Example**: $200,000 net income as sole proprietor: SE tax on full $200,000
Same income via S-Corp: $80,000 salary (SE tax on $80,000) + $120,000 distribution (no SE tax)
SE tax savings: ~$18,000/year (less S-Corp administrative costs ~$1,500–$3,000/year)

*Note: Tax rules for self-employed individuals are complex. Work with a CPA familiar with small business taxation for advice specific to your situation.*
