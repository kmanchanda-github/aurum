# Retirement Account Types

Understanding the differences between retirement account types is essential for tax-efficient investing.

## 401(k) Plans

Employer-sponsored retirement plans. 

**Traditional 401(k)**:
- Contributions are pre-tax (reduce taxable income now)
- Growth is tax-deferred
- Withdrawals in retirement taxed as ordinary income
- 2024 contribution limit: $23,000 ($30,500 if age 50+)
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) starting at age 73

**Roth 401(k)**:
- Contributions are after-tax (no immediate tax deduction)
- Growth is tax-free
- Qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free
- Same contribution limits as Traditional 401(k)

**Employer Match**: Many employers match a percentage of your contributions — this is essentially free money. Always contribute at least enough to capture the full match.

## Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)

**Traditional IRA**:
- Contributions may be tax-deductible (depends on income and whether you have a workplace plan)
- Growth is tax-deferred
- Withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
- 2024 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)

**Roth IRA**:
- Contributions are after-tax (no deduction)
- Growth and qualified withdrawals are completely tax-free
- Income limits apply: phases out above ~$146K (single) / ~$230K (married) for 2024
- No RMDs during your lifetime
- Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn penalty-free anytime

## Traditional vs. Roth: Which to Choose?

**Choose Traditional if**: You're in a high tax bracket now and expect lower income in retirement.
**Choose Roth if**: You're in a low tax bracket now and expect higher income later, OR you want tax-free income flexibility in retirement.

When in doubt, younger earners often benefit more from Roth.

## Health Savings Account (HSA)

The "triple tax advantage" account:
1. Contributions are pre-tax
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

After age 65, non-medical withdrawals are taxed like traditional IRA (no penalty).
2024 limits: $4,150 individual / $8,300 family.

Must have a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) to contribute.

## General Strategy

1. Contribute to 401(k) up to employer match
2. Max out HSA (if eligible)
3. Max out Roth IRA (if eligible)
4. Return to 401(k) and max out remaining limit
5. Taxable brokerage account for additional investing
