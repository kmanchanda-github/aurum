# Backdoor Roth IRA Strategy

High earners who exceed the income limits for direct Roth IRA contributions can still access Roth benefits through a legal technique called the "backdoor Roth IRA." It's one of the most powerful tax strategies available to high-income professionals.

## The Problem: Income Limits

In 2024, you cannot directly contribute to a Roth IRA if your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) exceeds:
- **Single filers**: Phase-out starts at $146,000; fully phased out above $161,000
- **Married filing jointly**: Phase-out $230,000–$240,000

Traditional IRA contributions have no income limit, but the tax deduction phases out at high incomes if you have a workplace retirement plan.

## The Backdoor Roth Solution

The backdoor Roth is a two-step process using a legal loophole:

**Step 1**: Make a **non-deductible Traditional IRA contribution** (up to $7,000 in 2024; $8,000 if age 50+). Because you can't deduct it, this contribution has no income limit.

**Step 2**: **Convert the Traditional IRA to Roth IRA**. Since you contributed after-tax dollars, the conversion is not taxed (or nearly not taxed — see the pro-rata rule below).

The money is now in a Roth IRA, growing tax-free forever.

## The Pro-Rata Rule: The Catch

The backdoor Roth works cleanly only if you have **no other pre-tax IRA money** (Traditional IRA, SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA). If you do, the IRS requires you to calculate the taxable portion of your conversion proportionally.

**Example of the problem**:
- You have $50,000 in a pre-tax Traditional IRA
- You contribute $7,000 as a non-deductible IRA
- You convert the $7,000 to Roth

The IRS sees $57,000 total IRA assets, of which $7,000 (12.3%) is after-tax. Converting $7,000 means only 12.3% ($860) is tax-free; the other $6,140 is taxed as ordinary income.

**Solution**: If you have a 401k at work that accepts rollovers, roll the pre-tax Traditional IRA into the 401k first, then do the backdoor Roth.

## Step-by-Step Execution

1. Open (or confirm you have) a Traditional IRA at your brokerage
2. Contribute $7,000 (or $8,000 if 50+) as a non-deductible contribution
3. Do NOT invest the money yet — keep it in cash/money market
4. Wait a few days (to avoid ambiguity with regulators)
5. Convert to Roth IRA (at the same or different brokerage)
6. File Form 8606 with your tax return to document the non-deductible contribution

## Form 8606: Critical Tax Filing

You must file **IRS Form 8606** every year you make a non-deductible IRA contribution or do a Roth conversion. This form tracks your basis (after-tax money) in Traditional IRAs. Without it, you may pay taxes on the conversion twice.

## Mega Backdoor Roth: The Advanced Version

Some 401k plans allow after-tax contributions beyond the regular $23,000 limit (up to $69,000 total in 2024), and allow in-plan Roth conversions or in-service withdrawals.

This "mega backdoor Roth" lets high earners move an additional $30,000–$40,000/year into Roth — but requires your specific 401k plan to permit it.

## Is Backdoor Roth Worth It?

**Benefits**:
- Tax-free growth forever (no RMDs in Roth)
- Tax-free withdrawals in retirement
- Flexibility — no required minimum distributions
- Estate planning advantages

**For most high earners**: Yes, absolutely worth doing annually. The $7,000/year adds up significantly over decades of tax-free compounding.

*Note: Roth conversion rules can be changed by Congress. Consult a CPA for tax advice specific to your situation.*
