# Tax-Advantaged Investment Accounts: 401k, IRA, HSA, 529

The US tax code offers powerful savings vehicles that let your investments grow with reduced or eliminated taxes. Using these accounts efficiently can add hundreds of thousands of dollars to your retirement savings.

## 401(k) and 403(b)

Employer-sponsored retirement plans. Contributions reduce your current-year taxable income.

**2024 contribution limits:** $23,000/year; $30,500 if age 50+.

**Traditional 401(k):** Pre-tax contributions, tax-deferred growth, taxed at ordinary income rates on withdrawal in retirement.

**Roth 401(k):** After-tax contributions, tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. Better if you expect higher tax rates in retirement.

**Employer match:** Free money. Always contribute enough to capture the full employer match — it's an immediate 50–100% return on that portion of your contribution.

## Traditional IRA

Individual Retirement Account. Anyone with earned income can contribute; deductibility depends on income and whether you have a workplace retirement plan.

**2024 limits:** $7,000/year; $8,000 if age 50+.

Contributions may be tax-deductible. Growth is tax-deferred. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.

**Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)** start at age 73.

## Roth IRA

Same contribution limits as Traditional IRA, but contributions are after-tax. Growth and qualified withdrawals are **completely tax-free**. No RMDs during the owner's lifetime.

**Income limits for 2024:** Phase-out begins at $146,000 (single) / $230,000 (married). Above these limits, use the "backdoor Roth" strategy.

Roth IRAs are particularly valuable for younger investors in lower tax brackets, and as a tax diversification tool in retirement.

## HSA (Health Savings Account)

Triple tax advantage — contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.

**2024 limits:** $4,150 (self-only) / $8,300 (family). Requires a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP).

After age 65, can withdraw for any purpose (taxed as ordinary income, like a Traditional IRA). This makes HSAs effectively a second IRA with a healthcare bonus.

## 529 College Savings Plan

After-tax contributions grow tax-free and withdrawals for qualified education expenses (tuition, room, board, books) are tax-free.

Many states offer additional state tax deductions for contributions. No income limits.

Starting in 2024, unused 529 funds can be rolled into a Roth IRA (up to $35,000 lifetime limit), reducing the risk of overfunding.

## Priority Order

Most financial advisors recommend this contribution priority:
1. 401(k) up to employer match (free money)
2. Max HSA if eligible
3. Max Roth IRA
4. Max 401(k) remaining contribution
5. Taxable brokerage for additional savings
