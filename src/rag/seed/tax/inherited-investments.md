# Inherited Investments and Step-Up in Basis

Inheriting investments comes with important tax rules that can save — or cost — significant amounts depending on how the assets are handled. The most powerful concept is the "step-up in basis," which can effectively eliminate capital gains taxes built up over a lifetime.

## What Is Cost Basis?

Cost basis is what you originally paid for an investment. When you sell, capital gains are calculated as:

**Capital Gain = Sale Price − Cost Basis**

A stock bought for $10 and sold for $100 has an $90 capital gain. That gain is taxed.

## The Step-Up in Basis

When someone dies and leaves you an investment, the IRS gives you a new cost basis — **the fair market value on the date of death**. This "steps up" the basis from what the deceased paid to what the asset is worth when you inherit it.

**The power of this rule**:
- Deceased bought Amazon stock in 2000 for $3/share
- At death in 2024, Amazon trades at $180/share
- You inherit the shares
- Your cost basis = **$180/share** (not $3)
- If you sell the day after for $182/share: you owe tax on $2/share, not $177/share

**Decades of gains are permanently erased from a tax perspective.** This is one of the most generous provisions in the US tax code.

## What Types of Accounts Get Step-Up?

**Gets step-up in basis**:
- Taxable brokerage accounts
- Individual stocks and bonds held directly
- Real estate
- Mutual funds in taxable accounts

**Does NOT get step-up**:
- Traditional IRA / 401k (distributions are fully taxable as ordinary income)
- Roth IRA (tax-free already — step-up is moot)
- Annuities (gains are ordinary income)

This distinction is critical for estate planning: assets in taxable brokerage accounts pass capital gains tax-free via step-up; IRAs pass the full tax burden to heirs.

## Inherited IRA Rules (Post-SECURE Act 2.0)

IRAs inherited from non-spouse beneficiaries (children, grandchildren) after January 1, 2020 are subject to the **10-Year Rule**: the entire account must be emptied within 10 years of the original owner's death.

Key implications:
- No more "stretch IRA" over a lifetime
- Large traditional IRA balances create massive income in years 1–10 for heirs
- Strategic Roth conversions during the owner's lifetime reduces the inherited IRA burden

**Spouse exceptions**: A surviving spouse can treat the inherited IRA as their own, roll it into their own IRA, and take RMDs based on their own age.

**Eligible designated beneficiaries** (minor children of the deceased, disabled/chronically ill individuals, those not more than 10 years younger) have different rules — consult a tax professional.

## Practical Steps When Inheriting Investments

1. **Don't sell immediately**: First, understand the cost basis reset. You likely owe little or no capital gains tax on any gain up to the date of death.

2. **Get a valuation date**: The step-up basis is typically the date of death, but an alternate valuation date (6 months later) can be elected for estate tax purposes if the estate is large.

3. **Retitle the accounts**: Work with the brokerage to transfer assets into your name. Each institution has its own paperwork process.

4. **Understand the RMD rules for inherited IRAs**: Missing RMD requirements triggers a 25% penalty.

5. **Consider strategic selling**: With stepped-up basis, selling inherited appreciated assets in the year of inheritance may incur no capital gains. This can be the optimal moment to diversify a concentrated position.

## Estate Planning Implications

The step-up in basis creates a strong incentive to **hold appreciated assets until death** rather than selling during life. Wealthy individuals often plan to:
- Keep appreciated stocks in taxable accounts (will get step-up)
- Gift low-basis assets to charity (no capital gains, full deduction)
- Use IRAs for assets not expected to appreciate dramatically (no step-up benefit anyway)

*Note: Tax laws governing inherited assets are complex and subject to change. Consult a CPA or estate planning attorney for your specific situation.*
