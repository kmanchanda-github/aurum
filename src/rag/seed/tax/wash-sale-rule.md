# The Wash Sale Rule

The wash sale rule is an IRS rule that prevents investors from claiming a tax loss when they sell a security and quickly repurchase the same or a substantially identical security. It's one of the most commonly misunderstood tax rules for active investors.

## What Is a Wash Sale?

A wash sale occurs when you:
1. Sell a security at a loss, AND
2. Within the **30 days before or after** that sale, buy the same or substantially identical security

The 61-day window (30 days before + day of sale + 30 days after) is what catches many investors off guard. You can trigger a wash sale by buying the replacement shares BEFORE selling the losing position.

## The Consequence

If a wash sale occurs, the IRS **disallows** the capital loss — you cannot claim it on your taxes. However, the disallowed loss isn't permanently gone. It's added to the **cost basis** of the replacement shares, effectively deferring the loss until you sell those shares.

## Example

You own 100 shares of XYZ stock, purchased at $50/share ($5,000 cost basis). The stock falls to $35/share (worth $3,500).

**Wash sale scenario**:
- December 15: You sell all 100 shares for $3,500 → $1,500 loss
- December 28: You buy 100 shares of XYZ again at $34/share ($3,400)

The $1,500 loss is **disallowed**. Your new cost basis = $34 + $15 (deferred loss) = **$49/share**.

When you eventually sell those shares at $49, you recognize no gain. Your loss wasn't eliminated — it was deferred.

**Clean scenario**:
- December 15: Sell XYZ for $1,500 loss
- January 16 (31 days later): Buy XYZ again

The $1,500 loss is **allowed**. You can use it to offset capital gains or up to $3,000 of ordinary income.

## What Counts as "Substantially Identical"?

The IRS hasn't provided a comprehensive definition, but these are considered substantially identical:
- The same stock or bond in the same company
- Options to buy the same stock you just sold
- Futures contracts on the same securities

**These are NOT substantially identical** (safe to buy within 30 days):
- A different company in the same sector (sell NVIDIA, buy AMD)
- A different S&P 500 ETF (sell SPY, buy IVV or VOO) — *this is debated but commonly practiced*
- A bond fund vs. a stock fund
- The same fund in a different fund family (sell Vanguard S&P 500, buy Fidelity S&P 500) — *also debated*

## Tax-Loss Harvesting Without Wash Sales

The most common tax-loss harvesting strategy:
1. Sell Fund A at a loss (e.g., sell VTSAX — Vanguard Total Stock Market)
2. Immediately buy a similar but not substantially identical fund (e.g., FSKAX — Fidelity Total Market)
3. After 31 days, switch back if desired

This captures the tax loss while staying fully invested.

## Wash Sales Across Accounts

The wash sale rule applies **across all your accounts**, including:
- Joint accounts with a spouse
- IRAs and Roth IRAs (but losses inside IRAs aren't deductible anyway)
- Taxable accounts at different brokerages

Selling at a loss in your taxable account and buying the same stock in your IRA within 30 days = wash sale.

## Tracking Wash Sales

Your brokerage tracks wash sales within your account and reports them on Form 1099-B. If you hold accounts at multiple brokers, you're responsible for tracking cross-account wash sales yourself — brokers don't coordinate with each other.

*Note: Tax law is complex. Consult a CPA for advice specific to your situation.*
